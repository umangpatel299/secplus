01Threat Actors OverviewWho Attacks · Why · How

A threat actor is any individual, group, or entity that poses a risk to an organization's security. Understanding threat actors helps defenders prioritize controls, allocate budgets, and design defenses appropriate for the most likely attackers against their specific organization.

Why Threat Actor Identification Matters

# Threat model drives your defense strategy: Small accounting firm: Most likely: organized crime (ransomware for financial gain) Less likely: nation-state (you don't have state secrets) Defense focus: backups, email security, patch management Hospital: Likely: organized crime (patient data = high value) nation-state (medical research = espionage target) Critical: ransomware + IoMT security + HIPAA compliance Government contractor: Likely: nation-state APT (you have cleared personnel + defense secrets) Defense focus: CMMC compliance, strict access controls, CI security Critical infrastructure (power, water): Likely: nation-state (sabotage/warfare capability) Defense focus: OT/ICS security, air gaps, NERC CIP # Key insight: knowing the actor tells you what controls matter most

Key Attributes to Know for Each Actor

Sophistication / Capability

What technical skill level does this actor have? Can they develop zero-days? Do they use off-the-shelf tools? Do they have persistent access teams? Shapes: what defenses are effective against them.

Resources / Funding

How much money and time can this actor dedicate to an attack? Nation-states: effectively unlimited. Script kiddies: near zero. Resources determine persistence and scale of attack.

Motivation / Intent

What does this actor want? Financial gain, espionage, disruption, ideology? Motivation determines targets, tactics, and what data/systems are at risk. Critical for threat modeling.

Nation-State / APT
Advanced Persistent Threat · State-Sponsored
HIGHEST SOPHISTICATION
SophisticationMaximum
ResourcesUnlimited
PatienceYears
Primary GoalEspionage
Definition Hackers working directly for or sponsored by a government. The most sophisticated and well-resourced threat actors. Operate with near-unlimited budgets, teams of specialists, and government intelligence resources. Sometimes called APTs — Advanced Persistent Threats — because they maintain long-term, stealthy access rather than smash-and-grab attacks.
Motivations Espionage (steal classified/military/industrial secrets), sabotage (damage critical infrastructure), pre-positioning (establish access for future use in wartime), influence operations (election interference, disinformation), economic advantage (steal R&D to benefit domestic industry).
Targets Government agencies, defense contractors, critical infrastructure (power grids, water, financial systems), advanced technology companies (semiconductors, aerospace, pharma), political organizations, think tanks, universities with classified research.
Tactics / TTPs Zero-day exploits (purchased or developed in-house), supply chain attacks (SolarWinds), spear phishing with extensive OSINT, custom malware families, watering hole attacks, living-off-the-land (use legitimate tools to avoid detection), long dwell time (months to years without detection).
Notable Examples Russia (APT28/Fancy Bear — 2016 election), China (APT41 — intellectual property theft), North Korea (Lazarus Group — financial theft + Sony Pictures), Iran (OilRig — Middle East energy), SolarWinds 2020 (Russia — ~18,000 orgs compromised).
APT Explained Advanced: custom tools, zero-days, deep technical capability. Persistent: stay hidden for months/years, patient, re-establish access if detected. Threat: clear adversarial intent, targeted rather than opportunistic. Nation-states and sophisticated cybercriminal groups both qualify as APTs.
NSNation-State: Deep DiveAPT Lifecycle

The APT Attack Lifecycle

# Nation-state APT doesn't smash and grab — methodical, patient: Phase 1 — RECONNAISSANCE (months): OSINT on target org and employees Identify: technology stack, key personnel, supply chain vendors Social media: who uses what tools, who travels when, org chart Phase 2 — INITIAL ACCESS (careful, often via supply chain or spear phish): Spear phish targeting specific employee with relevant context OR: supply chain compromise (SolarWinds model) OR: zero-day against internet-facing service Goal: minimal footprint — avoid detection Phase 3 — ESTABLISH FOOTHOLD: Install custom backdoor / implant Test: C2 (command and control) connectivity Maintain: multiple redundant persistence mechanisms Phase 4 — ESCALATE PRIVILEGE: Move from user → admin → domain admin Credential theft: Mimikatz, pass-the-hash Kerberoasting, DCSync to get domain credentials Phase 5 — LATERAL MOVEMENT (slow, deliberate, low noise): Move to high-value systems without triggering alerts Use legitimate tools: PsExec, WMI, RDP (blend into normal traffic) Map: what data exists, where is it, who has access Phase 6 — EXFILTRATION (slow, encrypted, small chunks): Compress + encrypt data → stage in inconspicuous location Exfil over long period via allowed protocols (HTTPS, DNS) Blend with normal traffic patterns Phase 7 — MAINTAIN ACCESS (years): Remain hidden after objectives met Re-entry: if discovered, multiple backdoors ensure return Average dwell time: 204 days (industry average)

Organized Crime
Cybercriminal Groups · Ransomware Gangs · Financial Fraud
FINANCIAL MOTIVE
SophisticationHigh
ResourcesHigh
PatienceMedium
Primary GoalMoney
Definition Professional criminal organizations operating online for financial profit. Run like businesses — with HR, technical support desks, affiliate programs (Ransomware-as-a-Service), and customer service for ransom negotiations. Often operate from jurisdictions where they're unlikely to be extradited.
Motivations Purely financial. Ransomware (encrypt files → demand payment), data theft (sell on dark web markets), business email compromise (BEC — trick finance into wire transfers), credit card fraud, cryptojacking (use victim's resources to mine cryptocurrency), banking trojans.
Targets Anyone who has money or valuable data — opportunistic. High-value: healthcare (HIPAA fines increase ransom pressure), financial services, manufacturing (high downtime cost), education, local/state government (understaffed IT, critical services). Ransomware: targets based on ability to pay AND critical nature of operations.
Tactics / TTPs Phishing campaigns at scale, RaaS (Ransomware-as-a-Service), initial access brokers (buy network access from IABs), double extortion (encrypt AND threaten to publish), BEC targeting CFO/finance, credential stuffing attacks, dark web data sales.
Notable Examples LockBit (RaaS — most prolific 2022-2024), Conti (healthcare — $180M 2021), REvil/Sodinokibi (Kaseya 2021, Colonial Pipeline-adjacent), FIN7 (financial fraud — Carbanak bank heist $1B), Evil Corp (financial trojans from Russia).
OCOrganized Crime Deep DiveRaaS · BEC · Double Extortion

Ransomware as a Service (RaaS)

# RaaS — crime organized like a business franchise: RaaS Operators (the developers): Build and maintain ransomware platform Provide: malware, C2 infrastructure, payment portal, negotiation support, decryption service Take: 20-30% of each ransom payment Affiliates (the attackers): Purchase access to RaaS platform Handle: initial compromise, lateral movement, deployment Keep: 70-80% of ransom payment No coding required — plug-and-play crime Initial Access Brokers (IABs): Specialize in gaining initial access Sell: VPN credentials, compromised admin accounts Price: $50-$100,000 depending on org size and access level Affiliates buy access → deploy ransomware # Double extortion evolution: Original: encrypt files → pay for decryption key Double: encrypt files + steal data → pay or we publish Triple: encrypt + steal + DDoS + contact customers directly # Why this model is effective: Healthcare: pay quickly — patient safety Government: pay to restore services Manufacturing: every hour offline = massive revenue loss

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

📧
BEC — The $2.7 Billion Scam
Attackers compromise or spoof executive email accounts to trick employees into making fraudulent wire transfers or gift card purchases. Classic: attacker poses as CEO to CFO — "I need you to wire $250K to this account urgently for a confidential acquisition. Don't discuss with anyone." FBI: BEC is the highest-loss cybercrime — $2.7B in 2022. Defense: dual-approval for wire transfers, out-of-band verification, no "urgent secrecy" bypass of process.

Hacktivists
Ideology-Driven · Social/Political Agenda · Anonymous
IDEOLOGY MOTIVE
SophisticationLow–Medium
ResourcesLow
PatienceShort
Primary GoalPublicity
Definition Hackers motivated by a political, social, or ideological cause rather than financial gain. "Hacking as activism." Want to: embarrass targets, disrupt operations, make a political statement, raise awareness of their cause. Often loosely organized — Anonymous being the most famous example.
Motivations Ideological — environmental, political, social justice, anti-government, anti-corporate, anti-war. Goal: embarrass the target, disrupt their operations, leak embarrassing information, or make a public statement. They want publicity and attention for their cause more than financial gain.
Targets Organizations they disagree with politically or ethically: corporations they view as corrupt, government agencies involved in controversial activities, media organizations, banks, oil companies, religious organizations. Opportunistic within their cause — whoever makes the best political statement.
Tactics / TTPs DDoS attacks (most common — Low Orbit Ion Cannon/LOIC), website defacement (replace homepage with political message), doxing (publish personal info of targets), data leaks (steal and publish embarrassing documents), social media campaigns to amplify attacks.
Notable Examples Anonymous (widespread DDoS/defacement — HBGary, Church of Scientology, governments), LulzSec (Sony, FBI, CIA), Killnet (pro-Russia DDoS — Ukraine war), Sudan (pro-Sudanese DDoS), WikiLeaks (data leaking — different but related model).
HVHacktivists Deep DiveDDoS · Defacement · Doxing

Hacktivist Tactics

DDoS Attacks

Overwhelm a website or service with traffic until it goes offline. Easy to participate — low technical barrier. LOIC (Low Orbit Ion Cannon): GUI tool anyone can run. Hivemind DDoS: coordinate via IRC/Telegram. Makes a statement: "look what we can do." Mitigation: DDoS protection services (Cloudflare, Akamai).

Website Defacement

Deface the target's homepage with political message. High visual impact — media coverage. Exploits CMS vulnerabilities (WordPress, Drupal) or SQL injection. Goal: embarrass the org, spread the message. Defense: WAF, patching, file integrity monitoring on web server.

Data Leaks / Doxing

Steal and publish sensitive documents or personal information. Embarrass leadership, expose wrongdoing. Doxing: publish private information (home address, phone, family) of specific individuals. Chilling effect on targets. Defense: DLP, encryption, access control on sensitive data.

// Real World — Anonymous vs HBGary Federal (2011)
# HBGary Federal CEO claimed he could unmask Anonymous leaders: Response: Anonymous compromised HBGary Federal systems Stolen: 70,000 emails → published on internet Content: embarrassing internal communications, client info CEO resigned within days Company folded What Anonymous used: SQL injection on HBGary's CMS website Social engineering HBGary's SysAdmin (via phone) Password reuse: same password as leaked site → corporate email Lesson: hacktivists motivated by retaliation + ideology Defense: patch CMS, unique passwords, MFA, don't provoke them

Primary tactics →DDoS (most common), website defacement, doxing, data leaks
Hacktivist goal →Publicity and attention for their cause — embarrass target — political statement
Anonymous →Most famous hacktivist group — loosely organized — DDoS and defacement campaigns

Insider Threats
Employees · Contractors · Trusted Insiders · Most Dangerous
TRUSTED ACCESS
SophisticationVariable
ResourcesLow–Med
AccessLegitimate
DetectionHardest
Definition Current or former employees, contractors, partners, or anyone with legitimate authorized access to systems and data who poses a security risk. The most difficult threat to defend against because they start with legitimate access and their activity often looks like normal work. Insider threat ≠ always malicious — negligence is also a major category.
Three Types Malicious Insider: intentionally harms org — theft, sabotage, espionage. Negligent Insider: accidental — clicks phishing link, loses laptop, misconfigures system, weak password. Compromised Insider: legitimate account taken over by external attacker — actions appear to be from trusted employee.
Motivations Financial (selling data or access), revenge (disgruntled — fired, passed over for promotion, workplace conflict), ideology (leaking to press, competitor), coercion (forced by external actor or organized crime), curiosity (snooping on colleagues' data), ego (proving they can access restricted data).
Why So Dangerous Already inside security perimeter — no need to breach external controls. Has legitimate access to resources — hard to distinguish malicious activity from normal work. Knows where valuable data is and how to access it. May have physical access to hardware. Can disable or circumvent security controls. Traditional perimeter defenses irrelevant.
Warning Signs Mass data download before resignation, accessing data outside normal job scope, unusual hours accessing sensitive systems, disgruntlement (conflicts, discipline, passed over), financial stress, recent job offer from competitor, accessing competitor or job search sites from work.

Detecting Insider Threats

# Technical controls for insider threat detection: User Behavior Analytics (UBA/UEBA): Baseline: learn normal behavior patterns per user Alert: deviation from baseline Example: "Alice normally downloads 5 files/day. Today: 50,000 files" Example: "Bob logged in from Moscow — he's normally in Toronto" DLP (Data Loss Prevention): Monitor: what data is leaving and by what channel Block or alert: sensitive data in email, USB, cloud upload Pattern matching: SSNs, credit cards, classified markings Privileged Access Management (PAM): Session recording: all admin sessions recorded Just-in-time: access granted only when needed, then revoked Approval workflow: admin actions require second-party approval Access reviews: Quarterly: managers certify what their employees still need Separation of duties: critical actions require two people Least privilege: people only have what their job requires # Non-technical indicators to watch for: HR flag: formal discipline, performance issues, resignation notice Finance: sudden financial stress, legal troubles Access: requesting access outside normal scope Network: visiting job search sites, competitor pages on work systems
// Scenario — The Disgruntled IT Admin
# IT admin loses promotion → become security risk: Timeline: Week 1: Admin learns junior colleague got promotion they expected Week 2: Admin visits job search sites from work computer Week 3: Admin begins copying server configs to personal Dropbox Week 4: Admin accepts job offer — gives 2-week notice Day before last: Admin deletes critical virtual machines Last day: Admin uses still-active VPN credential → exfils data What would have caught it: UBA alert Week 3: unusual Dropbox uploads from admin account DLP alert Week 3: config files being copied to personal cloud HR flag to security: performance issue + resignation = elevated risk Access revocation: VPN should have been disabled on last day Separation of duties: VM deletion should require second approval Lesson: insider threat requires people + process + technology No single technical control is sufficient

Unskilled Attackers
Script Kiddies · Low Skill · Opportunistic
LOWEST SOPHISTICATION
SophisticationVery Low
ResourcesMinimal
PatienceLow
ScaleVery High
Definition Individuals with limited or no technical skills who use pre-built tools, scripts, and exploit kits created by others. Also called "script kiddies." They don't understand how the attacks work — they just run the tools. Volume compensates for skill: automated scanning can hit millions of targets. Even basic defenses stop most of these attacks.
Motivations Thrill/bragging rights, curiosity, low-level financial gain (selling compromised accounts), attention/recognition in online communities, following tutorial videos ("I want to hack my ex's account"). Mixed — not purely financial or ideological. Sometimes they stumble into a more serious attack without realizing it.
How They Work Download pre-made exploit kits, use Metasploit without understanding it, run automated scanners (Shodan, Nmap), use leaked credential databases for credential stuffing, buy "hack tools" on dark web forums, watch YouTube/tutorial videos. Target selection: opportunistic — attack whoever is vulnerable, not specific targets.
Why They Still Matter Volume: millions of automated scans per day. Unpatched systems, default credentials, known CVEs — all easily exploited by unskilled attackers. You don't need sophistication to exploit a system that never changed its default router password. Basic hygiene (patches, no defaults, MFA) stops virtually all unskilled attacks.
SKUnskilled Attackers Deep DiveScript Kiddies · Tools · Defense

Tools Used by Unskilled Attackers

Automated Scanning

Shodan/Censys: finds internet-connected devices with known vulnerabilities or default credentials. Nmap: port scanner — finds open services. Nikto/WPScan: automated web vulnerability scanner. These require zero expertise to run — point at a target and click.

Pre-Built Exploit Kits

Metasploit Framework: pre-built exploits with GUI — "select target, select exploit, click execute." Published PoC code from CVE databases. Dark web exploit kits: pre-packaged attacks sold cheaply. They don't need to understand — they just run the tool.

Credential-Based Attacks

Credential stuffing: use leaked username/password databases. Password spraying: try common passwords against many accounts. Hydra/Medusa: brute force tools anyone can run. Leaked databases from Have I Been Pwned — billions of credentials available.

Defense is Simple

Patch known CVEs: unskilled attackers can't exploit what's patched. Change default credentials: they're looking for admin/admin. MFA: credential stuffing fails. Close unused ports: nothing to scan. Basic hygiene stops 99% of unskilled attacks.

// Exam Keywords

Script kiddie / unskilled →Uses others' tools — no custom capability — opportunistic — volume compensates for skill
Motivation →Mixed: thrill, curiosity, bragging rights, minor financial gain
Why they matter →High volume — hit millions of targets — basic defenses stop them
Defense →Patch CVEs + change defaults + MFA + close unused ports = stops virtually all unskilled attacks

Shadow IT
Unauthorized Technology · Unmanaged Risk · Not Always Malicious
INTERNAL RISK
IntentUsually Benign
Risk LevelHigh
VisibilityLow
ScaleWidespread
Definition IT systems, software, services, or devices that are used within an organization without explicit IT approval or oversight. NOT always malicious — usually employees trying to be more productive. The risk is: IT/security doesn't know about it, can't manage it, can't secure it, can't audit it. Classic: employees using personal Dropbox for work files because corporate OneDrive is "too slow."
Why It Happens IT processes too slow — employee needs a tool now, can't wait 3 weeks for approval. Approved tools don't meet needs — personal Gmail is easier than corporate email. Bring Your Own Device (BYOD) without proper MDM enrollment. Remote work: employees set up home systems outside IT's visibility. "I'll just use my own laptop for this — it's faster."
Examples Personal Dropbox/Google Drive with work files, unauthorized SaaS apps (Slack, Trello, Notion when not corporate-approved), personal laptops used for work, unapproved IoT devices connected to office network, personal email used for work communications, browser extensions that access work data.
Security Risks Data stored in unmanaged cloud = breach nobody knows happened. Unpatched personal devices = attack entry point. No MFA on personal services = easy credential theft. Data sovereignty: files stored in unapproved jurisdiction (GDPR concern). Compliance: auditors ask about data handling — shadow IT means you can't answer. No backup, no recovery, no DLP.
SITShadow IT Deep DiveDiscovery · Risk · Policy

Shadow IT Security Impact

# Real security consequences of shadow IT: Scenario 1 — The Personal Dropbox: Finance employee stores client contracts in personal Dropbox Personal Dropbox: no MFA enabled, reused password Password breach: Dropbox credentials in leaked database Attacker accesses: all client contracts — GDPR/contractual breach Organization: didn't know data was there → couldn't prevent → couldn't detect Scenario 2 — The Personal Laptop: Developer uses personal laptop because "it's faster" Personal laptop: no EDR, no patch management, no DLP Malware from pirated software: keylogger installed Keylogger captures: corporate VPN credentials, GitHub tokens Attacker: accesses corporate code repository Scenario 3 — The Rogue IoT: Marketing team buys smart TV for conference room without IT approval TV ships with old firmware, default credentials TV: sits on corporate network, never patched Attacker: uses TV as pivot point onto corporate network Common thread: IT/Security has NO visibility, NO control, NO ability to respond

Shadow IT Detection and Controls

Detection

  • CASB (Cloud Access Security Broker): discover what cloud services employees are using
  • Network monitoring: DNS queries to unapproved SaaS services
  • SIEM: unusual outbound connections to new cloud providers
  • DLP: data going to unauthorized destinations
  • Asset discovery: scan for unknown devices on network

Prevention

  • Approved tools list: fast approval process so employees don't go rogue
  • Web proxy: block access to unapproved SaaS categories
  • Endpoint policy: restrict unauthorized software installation
  • Network access control: only approved devices on corporate network
  • Security awareness: train employees on why shadow IT is risky

Solution →Fast approval process + CASB + web proxy + awareness training + NAC
08Attack MotivationsWhy They Attack

Understanding why a threat actor attacks helps predict what they will target, how they will attack, and when they will stop. Motivation drives everything from target selection to tactics to persistence.

💰
Financial Gain
The most common motivation. Attackers want money — directly or through theft of sellable assets. Includes: ransomware (pay or lose data), data theft (sell on dark web), BEC wire fraud, cryptojacking, banking trojans, credit card theft. Primary actors: organized crime groups. Defense relevance: financial actors stop when the money isn't worth the effort — make attacks economically expensive.
🔍
Espionage
Stealing secrets — government, military, corporate, or diplomatic. State espionage: steal classified documents, military blueprints, diplomatic cables. Corporate espionage: steal trade secrets, product designs, customer lists, business strategies. Can be: nation-state stealing from foreign governments OR corporations, or competitors hiring hackers. Primary actors: nation-states, sometimes organized crime hired by state actors.
💣
Disruption / Sabotage
Destroy, disrupt, or damage the target's operations — availability attacks. DoS/DDoS: make service unavailable. Destructive malware: wipe data, destroy systems (NotPetya 2017 — $10B damage). Physical infrastructure attacks: power grids, water treatment (Stuxnet). Ransom without intent to restore: pure destruction. Primary actors: nation-states (wartime, geopolitical), hacktivists (attention), terrorists.
✊
Ideology / Hacktivism
Attack driven by belief — political, social, religious, environmental. Want to: make a statement, embarrass the target, expose wrongdoing, advance a cause. Not primarily financial — willing to risk themselves for the cause. Can overlap with state actors (state uses ideology as cover). Primary actors: hacktivists (Anonymous), sometimes insider threats (whistleblowers).
📊
Data Exfiltration
Steal data for any subsequent use — sale, espionage, leverage, competitive advantage. Distinct from pure financial because the data itself is the prize, not a secondary asset. PII: sell for identity theft. IP: sell to competitor or government. Credentials: use to access more systems. Health data: HIPAA fine leverage. All major actor types exfiltrate data — they just use it differently.
😤
Revenge / Retaliation
Personal grievance drives the attack. Disgruntled employee attacks former employer after termination. Hacktivist retaliates after org attacks their cause (Anonymous vs HBGary). Nation-state retaliates for sanctions or cyber operations. Revenge attackers are particularly dangerous: they have inside knowledge and strong motivation. Defense: thorough offboarding + revoking all access immediately.
⚔️
Chaos / Anarchism
Attack just to cause chaos — no specific goal. Some hacktivists ("for the lulz"), some insiders with no specific agenda, some state actors who want to demonstrate capability or destabilize. NotPetya was a political weapon disguised as ransomware — no intent to let victims recover. Hard to reason about — no clear "what do they want" to negotiate with.
🌐
War / Geopolitical
Cyber operations as part of military or geopolitical strategy. Pre-positioning: establish access before conflict begins. Wartime cyber operations: disable enemy communications, power, finance. Psychological operations: spread disinformation. Hybrid warfare: combine cyber with kinetic operations. Increasing in relevance: Ukraine/Russia conflict shows cyber as integral component of modern warfare.
