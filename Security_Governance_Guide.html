01Security Governance OverviewDomain 5 — 20% of Exam

Security governance is the framework of rules, responsibilities, and processes that directs and controls how an organization manages its security program. It answers: Who decides security priorities? What rules must everyone follow? How do we prove we're compliant? How do we know controls are working?

Why Governance Matters

# Without governance — security chaos: Each team: sets their own security rules No consistency: IT does one thing, Dev does another No accountability: "who owns this security decision?" No audit trail: "prove you had controls in place" Regulators arrive: no policies, no documentation, massive fines Breach occurs: no policies = no legal defense = full liability # With governance — security structure: Policies: clear, documented, approved by leadership Standards: everyone uses same security requirements Procedures: step-by-step guides — nothing improvised Roles: everyone knows their security responsibility Accountability: violations have consequences Audit: documentation proves compliance Continuity: security doesn't leave when people do

The Governance Document Hierarchy

Mission & Vision
Policies — "What must be done"
Standards — "The specific requirements"
Guidelines — "Best practice recommendations"
Procedures — "Step-by-step instructions"
Baselines — "Minimum acceptable config"

Governance vs Management — Know the Difference

Governance — The "What and Why"

Sets direction and objectives. Board and executive level. Defines what security must achieve. Allocates resources and accountability. Ensures alignment with business strategy. Answers: "Are we doing the right things?"

Management — The "How"

Implements governance decisions. CISO and security team level. Executes policies and controls. Day-to-day security operations. Answers: "Are we doing things right?"

02The Document Hierarchy ExplainedPolicy · Standard · Guideline · Procedure

Each level of the governance hierarchy has a distinct purpose, audience, and authority level. Understanding the differences between policy, standard, guideline, and procedure is one of the most commonly tested concepts in Domain 5.

📋 POLICY High-level statement of management intent and direction. Mandatory — must be followed. Approved by senior leadership (CEO/Board). Does not specify technical details. Written for all employees. Mandatory
📐 STANDARD Specific, measurable requirements supporting a policy. Mandatory within its defined scope. Technical in nature — defines exact requirements. Owned by security team or IT. Mandatory
💡 GUIDELINE Recommended best practices and suggested approaches. NOT mandatory — advisory only. Provides flexibility for different situations. "Should" language vs "shall/must" in policies. Recommended
📝 PROCEDURE Step-by-step instructions for accomplishing a specific task. Operational level — for practitioners. Highly specific — tells exactly what to do and in what order. Should match policies and standards. Instructional
⚙️ BASELINE Minimum acceptable security configuration for a system or platform. Defines the secure starting point. CIS Benchmarks, STIG — external baselines. Internal baselines for specific systems. Minimum config

Concrete Example — Password Security Hierarchy

Policy (high-level mandatory directive): "All users must authenticate with strong credentials to access company systems. Passwords must meet complexity requirements as defined in the Password Standard." Approved by: CEO | Applies to: All employees Standard (specific measurable requirements): Password requirements: Minimum length: 12 characters Complexity: upper, lower, number, special char History: cannot reuse last 12 passwords Expiry: 90 days for standard accounts MFA: required for all remote access Service accounts: 20+ char, vault managed Guideline (recommended best practice): "Users SHOULD consider using a password manager to generate and store complex unique passwords. Passphrases of 4+ random words are recommended where possible." Note: Cannot be enforced — advisory only Procedure (step-by-step instructions): How to reset your Active Directory password: Step 1: Press Ctrl+Alt+Delete on your workstation Step 2: Click "Change a password" Step 3: Enter current password in first field Step 4: Enter new password meeting requirements (see Standard) Step 5: Confirm new password in third field Step 6: Click the arrow to confirm change Step 7: Log off and log on with new password to confirm

03Security Policies — Types & ContentAUP · BYOD · Data · IR · Remote

Policies are the foundation of a security program. Every control, procedure, and security activity should trace back to a policy. Without policies, there is no basis for enforcement, no defense in legal proceedings, and no consistent security posture.

Key Policy Types — Security+ Must Know

📄Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)All employees
Defines acceptable and unacceptable use of company systems, networks, and data. Employees sign on hire. Covers: personal use limits, prohibited activities, monitoring notice, consequences for violations. Foundation for disciplinary action when violated. Often the first policy employees encounter.
AUP typically covers: ✓ Permitted: work-related internet use, limited personal use ✗ Prohibited: downloading pirated content, illegal activities ✗ Prohibited: installing unauthorized software ✗ Prohibited: sharing credentials ⚠ Notice: "Company systems are monitored. Users have no expectation of privacy on company equipment." Enforcement: violations subject to disciplinary action
📱BYOD / Mobile Device PolicyEmployees with personal devices
Governs use of personal devices for work. Defines: what MDM controls apply to personal devices, what company can remotely wipe, data separation requirements, prohibited device configurations (jailbreaking). Must balance employee privacy with organizational security. Employees must explicitly consent before MDM enrollment.
BYOD policy elements: Enrollment: MDM required before accessing corporate email Privacy: company cannot access personal apps or photos Wipe: company can selectively wipe work data (personal data preserved on personal devices) Jailbreaking: prohibited — MDM will detect and block access Lost device: report within 24 hours for remote wipe
📊Data Classification and Handling PolicyAll employees
Defines how data is classified (Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted) and how each classification must be handled, stored, transmitted, and disposed of. Ensures sensitive data gets appropriate protection and employees know what to do with different types of data.
🚨Incident Response PolicySecurity team + all staff
Mandates that security incidents must be reported and defines the response framework. Establishes: incident reporting obligations, response team authority, communication requirements, management escalation thresholds. Without this policy, employees don't know they must report incidents.
🏠Remote Access / Telework PolicyRemote and hybrid employees
Defines requirements for working outside the office. Covers: VPN requirements, home network security expectations, device requirements, public Wi-Fi usage, physical security of company equipment. Became critical after COVID-19 normalized remote work.
Remote access requirements: VPN: required for access to internal resources Home network: WPA2 minimum, no default router password Device: corporate laptop or BYOD meeting MDM requirements Public Wi-Fi: VPN mandatory, no sensitive work on free Wi-Fi Physical: lock screen when unattended, clean desk Guests: do not allow others to view corporate work
🔐Password / Credential PolicyAll users
Sets requirements for password complexity, length, history, rotation, and MFA. References the Password Standard for specific technical requirements. Also covers: no password sharing, no writing down passwords, service account credential management.
🤝Vendor / Third-Party Security PolicyProcurement + IT + Legal
Defines security requirements for vendors and third parties who access company systems or handle company data. Requires: security questionnaires, right-to-audit clauses, data handling agreements, breach notification requirements. SolarWinds supply chain attack demonstrated why vendor policy matters.

04Standards, Procedures & FrameworksTechnical Requirements · Step-by-Step

While policies state what must be done, standards and procedures tell how specifically it must be done. Standards set measurable requirements; procedures provide the operational instructions.

Internal Standards — What They Define

Encryption Standard

Specific algorithms and key lengths required. Not just "encrypt data" — exactly which algorithm and how.

Data at rest: AES-256 minimum Data in transit: TLS 1.2+ minimum, TLS 1.3 preferred Prohibited: SSL, TLS 1.0, TLS 1.1, DES, RC4, MD5 Key management: HSM for critical keys Certificates: SHA-256 minimum, 2048-bit RSA min

Password Standard

Exact technical requirements for passwords. Supports the Password Policy with measurable specifics.

Standard users: 12 chars, complexity, 90-day expiry Admins: 16+ chars, 60-day expiry, MFA required Service accts: 20+ chars, vault-managed, no expiry Hash algorithm: bcrypt/Argon2 for stored passwords Lockout: 5 attempts → 15-minute lockout

Patching Standard

Defines exactly how fast vulnerabilities must be patched based on severity.

Critical CVE: 48 hours to patch High CVE: 7 days Medium CVE: 30 days Low CVE: 90 days Emergency: CISO approval required for exceptions

Configuration / Baseline Standard

Minimum security configuration for each platform and system type. References external standards where appropriate.

  • Windows servers: CIS Benchmark Level 1
  • Linux servers: CIS Benchmark Level 1
  • Network devices: DISA STIG or vendor hardening
  • Cloud: CIS Cloud Benchmark, provider security best practices

External Standards & Frameworks

🏛️
NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)
Voluntary US framework for managing cybersecurity risk. Five core functions: Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover. Widely used across industries. Non-prescriptive — tells you WHAT categories of activities, not HOW. CSF 2.0 (2024) added "Govern" function. Used as baseline for many organizational security programs.
📋
ISO/IEC 27001 — Information Security Management System (ISMS)
International standard for information security management. Certifiable — third-party auditors verify compliance. 93 controls in Annex A across 4 themes: Organizational, People, Physical, Technological. Requires documented ISMS with defined scope. ISO 27002 = implementation guidance for controls. Recognized globally — required by many enterprise contracts.
💳
PCI-DSS — Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard
Mandatory for any organization handling credit card data. 12 requirements covering network security, access control, monitoring, vulnerability management. Quarterly external scans by ASV required. Annual assessment — QSA for large merchants, SAQ for smaller. Non-compliance = fines + loss of ability to process cards.
🏥
HIPAA — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act
US law protecting patient health information (PHI). Administrative, Physical, and Technical Safeguards required. Security Rule + Privacy Rule + Breach Notification Rule. Annual risk assessments required. Business Associate Agreements (BAA) with all vendors handling PHI. OCR (HHS) enforces — fines up to $1.9M per violation category per year.
📊
SOC Reports — Service Organization Controls
AICPA audit reports for service organizations. SOC 1: financial reporting controls. SOC 2: security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, privacy (Trust Services Criteria). SOC 2 Type I: point-in-time / SOC 2 Type II: 6-12 month period. Type II much stronger evidence. Used by cloud providers and SaaS companies to demonstrate security posture to customers.

Procedures — Operational Documents

Onboarding Procedure

Step-by-step guide: create AD account, provision role-based access, enroll MFA, install required software, provide security training, sign required policies. Every new employee processed identically.

Incident Response Procedure

Specific steps for common incident types. Ransomware playbook, phishing response procedure, data breach procedure. Used by analysts under pressure — no improvisation needed.

Change Management Procedure

How to submit, review, approve, implement, and validate changes to production systems. Change Advisory Board (CAB) process. Rollback procedures included. Prevents unauthorized changes.

Backup and Recovery Procedure

Exact steps to perform backups, verify integrity, store offsite, and restore from backup. Tested regularly. When disaster hits — no guessing what to do.

Patch Management Procedure

How patches are tested, approved, scheduled, deployed, and verified. Emergency patch process. Exception handling when patch can't be applied. Closes the loop between vulnerability discovery and remediation.

Offboarding Procedure

Checklist: disable all accounts, collect devices, revoke certificates, transfer files, legal hold check, exit interview. Ensures no lingering access after departure.

05External ConsiderationsLegal · Regulatory · Industry · Geopolitical

Security governance doesn't exist in isolation — it must account for external requirements imposed by law, regulation, industry standards, contracts, and the geopolitical environment. Ignoring these can result in fines, lawsuits, loss of business, and criminal liability.

Legal and Regulatory Environment

⚖️ Data Privacy Laws GDPR (EU) — 72-hour breach notification, right to erasure, data minimization. CCPA (California) — consumer data rights, opt-out of data sale. PIPEDA (Canada) — private sector data protection. Organizations must comply with laws where their CUSTOMERS are, not just where the company is based. Operating globally = multiple overlapping laws.
🏥 Healthcare Regulations HIPAA (US) — PHI protection, BAAs with vendors, breach notification. HITECH Act — strengthened HIPAA enforcement, increased fines. State health privacy laws may be stricter than federal HIPAA. Governs: covered entities (hospitals, insurers) and business associates (IT vendors handling PHI).
💳 Financial Regulations PCI-DSS — card data. SOX — publicly traded companies, financial reporting integrity, IT general controls. GLBA (Gramm-Leach-Bliley) — financial institutions, customer financial data. FINRA — broker-dealers. Basel III — banks. Each adds security requirements for covered entities.
🏛️ Government Requirements FISMA — US federal agencies, NIST-based security controls, annual assessments. FedRAMP — cloud services to federal agencies, rigorous authorization process. DFARS/CMMC — defense contractors, NIST SP 800-171 controls required. State and local government regulations vary widely.
🌍 International & Cross-Border Data localization laws — some countries require data stored within their borders (Russia, China). Cross-border transfer restrictions — GDPR SCCs for transfers outside EU. Export controls (ITAR, EAR) — certain security technologies restricted from export. Jurisdictional complexity — which country's laws apply?

Industry-Specific Standards

Energy / Critical Infrastructure

NERC CIP — North American Electric Reliability Corporation Critical Infrastructure Protection. Mandatory for bulk electric system operators. Covers: physical security, electronic security perimeters, incident reporting, supply chain risk.

Defense / Government

CMMC — Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification. Defense contractors must achieve level 1, 2, or 3 based on contract sensitivity. Level 2 requires third-party assessment. Based on NIST SP 800-171 controls.

Automotive

TISAX — automotive industry information security standard (Germany/Europe). UN Regulation R155 — vehicle cybersecurity management. Increasingly relevant as vehicles become connected computers.

Contractual Obligations

# External obligations from contracts: Customer contracts: Enterprise customers may require: ISO 27001 cert, SOC 2 Type II, specific security controls, right-to-audit clauses, security questionnaire responses, pen test results Vendor/Partner agreements: SLAs: uptime guarantees, incident notification timelines Security Addendums: specific controls required of vendor BAAs: HIPAA Business Associate Agreements DPAs: GDPR Data Processing Agreements Cyber insurance requirements: Insurers increasingly require before coverage: MFA on all admin accounts Offline/immutable backups EDR on all endpoints Security awareness training Vulnerability scanning program Failure to maintain = policy void + claim denied! Cloud provider agreements: Shared responsibility model defined in ToS Data processing terms define your and their obligations Right to receive security logs and notifications

Geopolitical and Supply Chain Considerations

Geopolitical Risk

Some vendors banned or restricted by government: Huawei and ZTE restricted in US federal networks. TikTok restrictions in government devices. Russian software restrictions following sanctions. Chinese equipment concerns in critical infrastructure. Organizations must assess vendor country-of-origin risk.

Supply Chain Security

Vendors have access to your systems — their security = your risk. SolarWinds: malicious update from trusted vendor. Security questionnaires for all vendors. SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) requirements. Fourth-party risk — vendors' vendors. Right-to-audit clauses in contracts.

06Governance StructuresCommittees · Boards · Centralized vs Decentralized

Governance structures define how security decisions are made, who has authority, and how accountability is maintained. The right structure ensures security gets visibility at the right levels and decisions are made by the right people.

Governance Models

Centralized Governance

Single central security team sets policy for entire organization. Consistent standards everywhere. One approval authority. Easier to audit and demonstrate compliance.

  • + Consistent security posture
  • + Clear accountability
  • + Easier compliance demonstration
  • − May not fit all business unit needs
  • − Central team can become bottleneck
  • Best for: regulated industries, smaller organizations

Decentralized Governance

Business units have own security teams and policies. More flexibility. Faster local decisions. Risk: inconsistency, gaps, conflicting policies.

  • + Faster decisions at business unit level
  • + Better fit for diverse business needs
  • − Inconsistent security standards
  • − Harder to prove enterprise-wide compliance
  • − Duplication of security effort and cost
  • Best for: large conglomerates, highly diverse orgs

Hybrid / Federated Governance

Central team sets minimum standards — business units have flexibility within them. "Comply or explain" model. Most common in large enterprises. Balance of consistency and agility.

  • + Core standards enforced centrally
  • + Flexibility for unique business needs
  • + Scalable across diverse units
  • − More complex to manage
  • − Requires strong coordination
  • Best for: large enterprises, multinationals

Governance Bodies — Who Makes Decisions

🏛️
Board of Directors
Ultimate governance authority. Sets risk appetite and tolerance. Receives periodic security briefings (quarterly minimum for public companies). Approves major security investments and policies. Board-level cybersecurity committee increasingly common. SEC requires public companies to disclose board cybersecurity expertise. Approves the overall information security program.
👔
Executive Leadership / C-Suite
CEO owns overall accountability. CISO manages security program — reports to CEO, CTO, or Board depending on org. CFO approves security budget. CTO/CIO owns technology risk. COO handles operational continuity. CLO (Legal) handles regulatory compliance. All have security responsibilities in their domain.
🔒
Security Steering Committee
Cross-functional committee making security program decisions. Membership: CISO, CTO, CIO, Legal, HR, Business unit heads, Audit. Responsibilities: review and approve security policies, prioritize security investments, review compliance status, escalation point for major security decisions. Meets monthly or quarterly. Bridges security team and business.
🔄
Change Advisory Board (CAB)
Reviews and approves changes to production systems. Security represented on CAB to evaluate security impact of changes. Emergency CAB process for critical patches. Prevents unauthorized or risky changes. Documents rationale for all production changes — compliance evidence.
🔍
Internal Audit Committee
Independent review of security controls effectiveness. Reports to Board — not management (independence is critical). Conducts security audits, assesses policy compliance, reviews security incidents for lessons learned. Provides assurance to Board and external regulators. Should have no operational security responsibilities — pure oversight role.

Risk Appetite and Risk Tolerance

# Board establishes risk appetite — everything flows from this: Risk Appetite: The AMOUNT and TYPE of risk an organization is willing to ACCEPT Set by Board — applies to entire organization Example: "We will not accept risk of customer PII exposure above a 1-in-1000 probability of breach per year" Risk Tolerance: Acceptable VARIATION from risk appetite — the wiggle room More operational — "how far can we deviate before escalating?" Example: "CVSS Critical vulnerabilities: tolerance = 0 All must be patched within 48 hours — no exceptions" Risk Threshold: The level at which risk triggers ESCALATION or ACTION Breach of threshold = escalate to governance body Example: "If > 3 critical vulnerabilities unpatched after 48hrs = CISO notified + Board security committee briefed" # How it flows down: Board: sets risk appetite (high level) CISO: translates to security risk tolerance Security team: implements controls to stay within tolerance Audit: verifies organization operating within appetite

07Roles & ResponsibilitiesCISO · DPO · ISSO · All Staff

Security is everyone's responsibility — but different roles carry different specific obligations. Clear role definition prevents gaps ("I thought they were doing that") and overlaps ("three teams all doing the same thing inefficiently").

Executive Security Roles

CISO — Chief Information Security Officer
Owns the information security program. Strategic and operational security leadership.
  • Develop and maintain security strategy and program
  • Report to Board/CEO on security posture
  • Own security policies and standards
  • Manage security budget and team
  • Lead incident response at executive level
  • Interface with regulators and auditors
  • Translate security risk into business risk
  • Decision authority on major security investments
CTO / CIO — Technology Leadership
Owns technology infrastructure — security must be embedded in technology decisions.
  • Ensure security in technology architecture decisions
  • Approve major technology changes (security impact)
  • Own disaster recovery and business continuity
  • Resource security team appropriately
  • Balance security with operational agility
DPO — Data Protection Officer
Required by GDPR for certain organizations. Independent role — cannot have conflicting duties.
  • Monitor GDPR compliance
  • Advise on data protection impact assessments
  • Interface with data protection authority
  • Train staff on GDPR obligations
  • First point of contact for data subjects
  • Must be independent — cannot be fired for performing role
Privacy Officer
Manages privacy program — may overlap with DPO in GDPR organizations.
  • Develop and maintain privacy policies
  • Manage privacy impact assessments
  • Handle individual privacy rights requests
  • Interface with privacy regulators
  • Train staff on privacy obligations

Data Roles — Ownership and Stewardship

Data Owner: WHO: Senior business executive (not IT!) EXAMPLE: CFO owns financial data, CMO owns marketing data RESPONSIBILITIES: Decide data classification level Determine who has access Accept risk on behalf of organization Accountable for regulatory compliance Authorize data sharing with third parties Data Custodian (Data Steward): WHO: IT staff / database administrators EXAMPLE: DBA, sysadmin RESPONSIBILITIES: Implement owner's classification decisions Apply technical controls (encryption, access) Perform backups and ensure availability Technical implementation only — no policy decisions Follows directions from Data Owner Data Processor: WHO: Third party processing data on your behalf EXAMPLE: Payroll vendor, cloud provider GDPR TERM: organization processing data for controller REQUIRES: Data Processing Agreement (DPA) Data Controller: WHO: Your organization (determines purposes of processing) GDPR TERM: entity deciding WHY and HOW data is processed RESPONSIBLE: For processor's actions with your data MUST HAVE: DPA with every processor # Critical distinction for exam: Data Owner ≠ Data Custodian Owner: business decision maker — not necessarily technical Custodian: technical implementer — no policy authority

Operational Security Roles

Security Analyst (SOC)

Monitor alerts, investigate incidents, triage security events, execute playbooks, escalate confirmed threats. Tier 1/2/3 levels with increasing complexity. Eyes on glass — first responders to security events.

Security Architect

Design secure systems and networks. Create security reference architectures. Review designs for security flaws before build. Ensure security requirements translated into technical designs. Bridge between policy and implementation.

Vulnerability Manager

Own vulnerability management program. Run scanning tools, prioritize findings, track remediation, report on patch compliance. Liaison between security team and IT operations for patching.

ISSO — Information Systems Security Officer

Government/federal term. Owns security posture for a specific system. Maintains system security plan. Ensures ATO (Authorization to Operate) maintained. Primary security liaison for that system.

Compliance Manager

Manage regulatory compliance program. Coordinate audits, maintain evidence, track compliance status, manage remediation of findings. Interface with regulators and external auditors.

Incident Commander

Coordinates response during security incidents. Clear decision authority under pressure. Manages communication to stakeholders. Not necessarily most technical — must be decisive leader. Defined in IRP before incident occurs.

All Employees — Security Responsibilities

# Security is not just IT's job — everyone has obligations: All employees MUST: ✓ Complete annual security awareness training ✓ Report suspected security incidents promptly ✓ Follow Acceptable Use Policy ✓ Protect their credentials — never share ✓ Report lost or stolen devices immediately ✓ Lock workstation when leaving desk ✓ Not click suspicious links or open unexpected attachments ✓ Follow data classification and handling requirements ✓ Complete mandatory compliance training (HIPAA, PCI, etc.) Managers ADDITIONALLY: ✓ Ensure direct reports complete training ✓ Certify access during access recertification campaigns ✓ Report suspicious employee behavior (insider threat) ✓ Understand security policies applicable to their team ✓ Participate in business impact analysis for their function # "Security is everyone's responsibility" is not just a slogan # It has legal implications — employees can be held liable

08Governance Monitoring & ReviewMetrics · Audits · KPIs · Continuous

Governance without monitoring is just documentation. Effective governance requires continuous measurement of whether policies are being followed, controls are working, and the security program is achieving its objectives.

Security Metrics and KPIs

# Key governance metrics — what gets measured: Compliance metrics: Policy acknowledgment rate: target 100% annually Security training completion: target 100% by deadline Patch compliance rate: Critical: 100% in 48hrs Access recertification: 100% completed quarterly Exception count: track open exceptions Security operations metrics: Mean Time to Detect (MTTD): how fast breaches found Mean Time to Respond (MTTR): how fast breaches contained Vulnerability scan coverage: % of assets scanned Vulnerability remediation rate: % fixed within SLA Open critical vulns: target = 0 Risk metrics: Risk register items: count by severity Residual risk trend: improving over time? Security incidents: by type, severity, source Control effectiveness: audit findings trend Program metrics: Security budget utilization: planned vs actual Training effectiveness: phishing simulation click rates Vendor risk assessments: completed vs due Policy review cadence: all policies reviewed annually?

Audit Types

Internal Audit

Conducted by internal audit team — independent from security operations. Reviews: policy compliance, control effectiveness, regulatory adherence. Reports to Audit Committee/Board. Continuous audit model — not just annual. Provides management assurance.

External Audit

Conducted by independent third party. Most credible — no conflict of interest. Required for: SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001 certification, PCI-DSS QSA assessments. More expensive but required for many certifications and customer contracts.

Regulatory Examination

Conducted by regulator — OCR for HIPAA, OCC for banks, SEC for public companies. Not voluntary. Regulator has subpoena power. Findings can result in fines, consent decrees, business restrictions. Preparation is ongoing — not just before exam.

Penetration Test

Authorized simulated attack to verify control effectiveness. Technical audit of security controls. Required by: PCI-DSS (annual), many cyber insurance policies. Findings become action items with remediation deadlines. Different from vulnerability scanning — tests real exploitability.

Continuous Monitoring Program

📊
What Continuous Monitoring Covers
Technical monitoring (SIEM, EDR, vulnerability scanning), compliance monitoring (policy adherence, patch status, configuration drift), risk monitoring (risk register changes, new threats), vendor monitoring (third-party risk changes), and regulatory monitoring (new regulations and requirements that affect the organization). Results feed into governance reports and trigger policy/standard updates when gaps are found.

Policy Review and Update Cycle

# When policies should be reviewed and updated: Scheduled reviews (minimum annually): Every policy reviewed for currency Regulatory changes incorporated New threats/vulnerabilities addressed Lessons learned from incidents applied Triggered reviews (event-based): Major security incident ← review related policies New regulation published ← immediate review Significant technology change ← affected standards Merger/acquisition ← harmonize policy frameworks New business line ← extend policy scope Audit finding ← immediate remediation Key personnel change ← ensure continuity Policy review process: Policy owner: initiates review SME review: technical accuracy Legal review: regulatory compliance Stakeholder review: operational feasibility Approval: appropriate authority (CEO for company policy) Communication: all affected parties notified Training: if significant change — retrain employees

CISO initiates emergency policy update 2. Legal review: confirms SEC interpretation 3. Updated policy: "notify CISO within 1 hour, Board within 24hr, SEC disclosure assessment within 72 hours" 4. CEO approves updated policy 5. All executives trained on new requirement 6. IR playbooks updated to include SEC notification step 7. External counsel pre-briefed on process Result: compliant before SEC audit ✓ Without annual review: discovered during SEC investigation = fine

Scenario → Answer Cheat Sheet

// Exam Scenarios → Answers

"High-level mandatory directive from management"→ Policy — uses "shall/must" — approved by executive/Board
"Specific measurable technical requirements"→ Standard — mandatory within scope — more detailed than policy
"Recommended but not required best practice"→ Guideline — "should" language — advisory only — flexible
"Step-by-step instructions for IT staff"→ Procedure — operational level — specific steps in order
"Minimum acceptable security configuration"→ Baseline — CIS Benchmarks, STIG examples
"Document employees sign on first day"→ Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)
"Policy governing personal devices for work"→ BYOD Policy — MDM requirements, privacy, selective wipe
"Framework for all-sector security: Identify, Protect, Detect..."→ NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)
"International certifiable security management standard"→ ISO/IEC 27001 — ISMS — 93 controls
"Mandatory for organizations handling credit card data"→ PCI-DSS — 12 requirements — quarterly external scans
"EU regulation — 72-hour breach notification"→ GDPR — also requires DPO for many orgs
"US law protecting patient health information"→ HIPAA — Technical/Physical/Administrative safeguards
"Required for defense contractors — 3 levels"→ CMMC — Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification
"Financial reporting integrity for public companies"→ SOX — Sarbanes-Oxley — IT general controls required
"Audit report type showing 6-12 month period of controls"→ SOC 2 Type II — most credible for service organizations
"Who owns security program at highest level?"→ CISO — reports to Board/CEO — strategic and operational security
"Business executive deciding who can access data"→ Data Owner — NOT IT — business accountability
"IT staff implementing encryption as directed"→ Data Custodian — implements technical controls for Data Owner
"Required by GDPR — must be independent"→ Data Protection Officer (DPO)
"Government role owning security for specific system"→ ISSO (Information Systems Security Officer)
"Review security policy — when must it be done?"→ Annually minimum PLUS event-triggered (incidents, new regs)
"Audit that reports to Board — not management"→ Internal Audit — independence is critical — cannot report to CISO
"Amount of risk organization willing to accept"→ Risk appetite — set by Board
"Cross-functional group approving security policies"→ Security Steering Committee — CISO + business heads
