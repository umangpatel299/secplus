A1Audits & Assessments OverviewVerify · Evidence · Assurance

Audits and assessments provide independent verification that security controls are working as intended. They answer: are we actually doing what we say we're doing? Are our controls effective? Where are the gaps?

Assessment vs Audit — The Distinction

# Assessment vs Audit — often confused: ASSESSMENT: Purpose: Identify weaknesses and improve security posture Focus: Technical — what vulnerabilities exist? Tone: Collaborative — help you find and fix problems Output: Findings with remediation recommendations Examples: Vulnerability scan, risk assessment, pen test Who: Internal security team or external consultants AUDIT: Purpose: Verify compliance with policies, standards, regulations Focus: Process — are controls documented and followed? Tone: Independent — objective evidence review Output: Audit findings, opinion, compliance status Examples: SOC 2 audit, PCI-DSS QSA audit, internal IT audit Who: Independent auditors (cannot audit own work) # They complement each other: Assessments → find what to fix Audits → verify the fixes were made and sustained

Why Both Are Needed

Assessments Find Reality

Technical testing reveals actual security posture. Documentation says "MFA enabled" — pen tester finds MFA bypass. Policies say "patching within 14 days" — vulnerability scan shows 60-day-old critical CVEs. Assessments reveal the gap between stated and actual security.

Audits Ensure Sustainability

Compliance must be maintained — not just achieved once. Audits verify controls operate consistently over time. An audit finding tells you: this control failed to operate as required. Without audits, security can degrade between assessments without anyone noticing.

Audit →Verify compliance — independent — process focus — evidence review
Independence →Auditors cannot audit their own work — conflict of interest
A2AttestationFormal Declaration of Compliance

Attestation is a formal declaration — signed by an authorized person — that certain security controls or compliance requirements are met. It creates legal accountability: the person signing is personally responsible for the accuracy of the statement.

Types of Attestation

📜Self-AttestationInternal declaration
The organization itself declares compliance. CISO, CEO, or executive signs a statement confirming controls are in place. Used when: third-party audit not required, initial compliance claim, smaller-scope requirements. PCI-DSS SAQ (Self-Assessment Questionnaire) is a form of self-attestation for smaller merchants. Risk: no independent verification — organization could be mistaken or dishonest. Best backed by internal audit evidence.
🏢Third-Party AttestationIndependent verification
Independent auditor or assessor provides the attestation. Highest credibility — auditor has no conflict of interest. Examples: SOC 2 Type II report (CPA firm attests), PCI-DSS AoC with QSA signature, ISO 27001 certificate from accredited certification body. The attestor's professional license and reputation are on the line — significant accountability. Required for enterprise customers and regulated industries.
✍️Letter of AttestationFormal written statement
Formal written letter from a qualified person confirming specific facts. Examples: auditor's letter confirming controls reviewed, legal counsel letter confirming no known regulatory violations, CISO letter confirming security assessment was conducted. Often required as part of contract due diligence, M&A transactions, or regulatory filings. Creates paper trail of accountability.

Attestation in Practice

# What different attestations cover: SOC 2 Attestation (CPA firm): "Based on our examination, the description of [Company's] system fairly presents the system and the controls... are suitably designed and operating effectively throughout the period from Jan 1 to Dec 31, 2024" Signed by: CPA firm audit partner Liability: professional liability + potential criminal fraud PCI-DSS Attestation of Compliance (QSA): QSA certifies: assessed organization against PCI-DSS v4.0 Merchant certifies: information in the AoC is accurate Both sign: QSA + senior officer of assessed company HIPAA Risk Analysis Attestation (Internal): CISO attests: comprehensive risk analysis was conducted Documents: methodology, scope, findings, treatment Date and signature: establishes when analysis was done Required by: HHS audit request, breach investigation # Key principle: attestation creates accountability # False attestation: can be fraud / criminal liability

// Exam Keywords

Attestation →Formal signed declaration of compliance — creates legal accountability
Self-attestation →Organization declares its own compliance — no independent verification
Third-party attestation →Independent auditor certifies — highest credibility — SOC 2, ISO 27001
PCI AoC →Attestation of Compliance — signed by QSA + senior org officer
A3Internal AuditsIndependent · Continuous · Pre-audit

Internal audits are conducted by an organization's own audit staff — independent from the security team being audited. They provide ongoing assurance, find issues before external auditors do, and are a key governance control.

Internal Audit Characteristics

# What makes an audit truly "internal": Internal audit staff: employees of the organization Independence: separate department from security ops Internal auditors do NOT report to CISO Report to: Audit Committee (board level) or CFO / CEO — never to the auditee Why independence matters: Auditor cannot audit own work If security team audits itself: conflict of interest Board needs objective view of security posture External regulators won't accept non-independent audit Scope: typically assigned by Audit Committee Annual audit plan: covers all risk areas over 3-5 years Prioritized by risk: higher risk areas audited more frequently Triggered: by incidents, regulatory changes, board requests

Internal Audit Types

Compliance Audit

Verifies adherence to policies, standards, and regulations. "Are employees following the password policy?" "Is change management being followed for all production changes?" Compares actual practice to documented requirement. Output: gaps and non-conformities.

Operational Audit

Evaluates efficiency and effectiveness of security processes. "Is our patch management process taking 45 days when it should take 14?" "Is the IR process working?" Identifies process improvement opportunities beyond just compliance.

IT General Controls (ITGC) Audit

Specific to IT controls required for financial reporting (SOX). Access controls, change management, computer operations, IT security. Must be audited annually for public companies. Output feeds into external financial audit opinion.

Internal Audit Process

# Standard internal audit lifecycle: 1. Planning: Define scope and objectives Review prior audit findings (are old issues fixed?) Identify key risks to focus on Develop audit program (what to test and how) Notify auditee (usually 2-4 weeks notice) 2. Fieldwork: Request evidence: policies, configs, logs, reports Interview key personnel: controls in practice Test controls: sample transactions, configs Document: workpapers for every test performed Identify: preliminary findings 3. Reporting: Draft report: findings with risk rating and evidence Management response: auditee responds to each finding Final report: issued to Audit Committee / executive Contains: opinion, findings, management response, timeline 4. Follow-up: Track remediation of findings Verify: fixes were actually implemented Report status to Audit Committee quarterly Repeat: findings unresolved → escalate

Internal Audit — Value to Security Team

Pre-Audit Preparation

Internal audit acts as a "dress rehearsal" for external audits. Finds issues before the PCI QSA or HHS auditor finds them. Much better to fix gaps internally than get external audit findings. Security teams should actively engage with internal audit as a partner.

Continuous Assurance

Unlike annual external audit, internal audit provides year-round assurance. Regular checks catch control degradation early. "We were compliant in January — are we still compliant in October?" Internal audit answers this continuously.

Internal audit value →Finds issues before external auditors — "dress rehearsal" for compliance audit

A4External AuditsIndependent · Credible · Regulatory

External audits are conducted by independent third parties with no connection to the organization. They provide the highest level of assurance credibility because the auditor has no conflict of interest — their reputation and license are at stake.

Types of External Audits

📊SOC 2 AuditCPA firm · SaaS vendors · Annual
Conducted by licensed CPA firm. Covers Trust Service Criteria: Security, Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, Privacy. Type I: design at point in time. Type II: operating effectiveness over 6-12 months — gold standard. Report shared with customers under NDA. Required by enterprise buyers for cloud/SaaS vendors. Cost: $30K-$150K+ depending on scope.
💳PCI-DSS AssessmentQSA · Level 1 merchants · Annual
Conducted by Qualified Security Assessor (QSA) — PCI SSC certified. Required for Level 1 merchants (>6M transactions/year). Reviews all 12 PCI-DSS requirements. Output: Report on Compliance (ROC) + Attestation of Compliance (AoC). Quarterly ASV external scans also required. Non-compliance: losing card processing ability.
🌍ISO 27001 Certification AuditAccredited CB · International · 3-year cycle
Conducted by accredited Certification Body (CB). Stage 1: documentation review. Stage 2: implementation audit — verify controls working. Certificate valid 3 years with annual surveillance audits. Scope-limited: organization defines what's in scope. Used as B2B trust signal — "we have ISO 27001" proves security commitment to partners.
🏥Regulatory ExaminationGovernment agency · Healthcare/Finance/Energy
HHS OCR audits healthcare orgs for HIPAA compliance. SEC examinations for financial firms. FDIC for banks. CISA for critical infrastructure. Cannot refuse — regulators have legal authority. Findings: corrective action plans with deadlines. Unresolved findings: escalate to fines and enforcement actions. Unannounced inspections possible.

Audit Findings — Classification

# Audit findings are graded by severity: Critical Finding: Control completely absent or fundamentally broken Example: "No MFA on any admin accounts" Immediate remediation required before audit closes May result in audit opinion modification High/Major Finding: Significant control gap — material impact if exploited Example: "Critical patches over 60 days old on 30% of systems" Remediation plan required with specific timeline Medium/Moderate Finding: Control exists but effectiveness limited Example: "Access reviews performed but no evidence of revocations" Remediation within agreed timeframe Low/Minor/Observation: Best practice not followed — low risk Example: "Password policy doesn't specify complexity" Note for improvement — no strict timeline # External audit opinion types (SOC 2): Unqualified ("clean"): all controls operated effectively ✓ Qualified: most controls OK — specific exceptions Adverse: material weaknesses in controls Disclaimer: couldn't obtain sufficient evidence

Preparing for External Audits

Before the Audit

  • Run internal audit as rehearsal
  • Collect and organize evidence in advance
  • Fix known gaps before auditors arrive
  • Train staff who will be interviewed
  • Establish single point of contact for auditors
  • Review prior year findings — are they all fixed?
  • Prepare evidence repository with read-only access

During the Audit

  • Provide evidence promptly — delays = red flags
  • Don't guess — say "I'll find that for you"
  • All auditor requests go through point of contact
  • Document everything you provide
  • Challenge findings factually with evidence
  • Resolve preliminary findings before final report
  • Treat auditors as partners not adversaries

QSA →Qualified Security Assessor — PCI-DSS Level 1 auditor — certified by PCI SSC
Unqualified opinion →"Clean" SOC 2 — all controls operated effectively — what you want
Qualified opinion →Most controls OK but specific exceptions found — red flag for customers
Regulatory examination →HHS/SEC/FDIC — legal authority — cannot refuse — unannounced possible
A5Penetration TestingAuthorized · Exploit · Prove

Penetration testing is authorized, simulated cyberattacks against systems to find and demonstrate exploitable vulnerabilities. Unlike vulnerability scanning, pen testers actively try to exploit what they find — proving real-world impact, not theoretical risk.

Pen Test Phases

1
Reconnaissance
Passive + active info gathering. OSINT, DNS, ports, employees, technologies
2
Scanning
Port scanning, service enumeration, vulnerability identification
3
Exploitation
Actively exploit vulnerabilities. Gain initial access. Prove real-world impact
4
Post-Exploitation
Lateral movement, privilege escalation, persistence, data access
5
Reporting
Document findings, risk ratings, evidence, remediation guidance

Pen Test Types

# Black / Grey / White box — what information is given: Black Box: No prior knowledge — company name only Simulates: external unknown attacker Realistic: yes — but slow (time on recon) Finds: externally exploitable vulnerabilities Best for: external attack surface assessment Grey Box: Partial knowledge — credentials, network map Simulates: insider threat / compromised account Efficient: less time on recon → deeper testing Finds: what authenticated attacker can access Best for: internal network, application testing White Box: Full knowledge — source code, architecture, all creds Simulates: deep technical review Thorough: no time wasted on guessing Finds: deepest vulnerabilities — code-level Best for: specific application security review

Specialized Pen Test Types

Network Pen Test

External: test attack surface visible from internet. Internal: assume attacker inside — how far can they go? Tests: firewall rules, segmentation, lateral movement paths, credential attacks. Most common pen test type.

Web Application Pen Test

Tests specific web applications for OWASP Top 10 vulnerabilities. SQL injection, XSS, broken authentication, IDOR, API security. Grey box preferred — use app as authenticated user. Manual + automated (Burp Suite). Annual minimum for customer-facing apps.

Social Engineering

Tests human vulnerabilities. Phishing simulation (email), vishing (voice calls), physical tailgating. Usually combined with technical test — realistic attacker uses both. Requires explicit authorization and employee notification policy. Results: click rates, reporting rates.

Red Team Exercise

Simulates advanced persistent threat (APT). Multi-week to multi-month engagement. Uses: phishing, exploitation, lateral movement, persistence — realistic attack chain. Blue team doesn't know when/how attacks come. Tests: detection AND response capability.

Physical Pen Test

Tests physical security controls. Tailgating into secure areas, bypassing badge access, social engineering receptionists. Tests: locked server room actually locked? CCTV blind spots? Waste disposal security? Rarely done but very revealing.

Cloud Pen Test

Tests cloud environment security configurations. IAM permissions, S3 bucket policies, network security groups, serverless function security. Must comply with cloud provider's pen test policies (AWS/Azure/GCP each have rules on what you can test).

Pen Test Report Structure

# Professional pen test deliverable: Executive Summary (non-technical): Overall security posture assessment Key risks in business terms (not technical jargon) Priority findings: "Attacker could steal all customer data" Comparison to last year (if applicable) Technical Findings (detailed): Each finding: Title: descriptive name Severity: Critical/High/Medium/Low/Info CVSS Score: if applicable Description: what the vulnerability is Evidence: screenshots, logs, exploit output Business Impact: what an attacker could do Affected systems: IP, hostname, URL Remediation: specific steps to fix Remediation Guidance: Prioritized list by severity Short-term (immediate): patch/disable/isolate Long-term (strategic): architecture improvements # Re-test: After org remediates findings → pen tester re-tests Verifies: fixes actually work (not just documented) Certificate of re-test: evidence of remediation
// Scenario — Pen Test Finds Critical Before Attacker Does
# Annual web application pen test — e-commerce platform: Day 2 of engagement (grey box): Tester authenticated as standard customer account Testing: access control on order history Request: GET /api/orders/order_id=10047 Response: order details for tester's own order ✓ Modified: GET /api/orders/order_id=10046 Response: another customer's order details! (IDOR) Tester iterates IDs: order_id=1 through 10047 → ALL accessible Contains: name, address, email, payment last 4, order history Count: 847,293 customer orders → all accessible by any user CRITICAL FINDING: Insecure Direct Object Reference (IDOR) CVSS: 9.1 (Critical) Impact: Full customer order history and PII of 847,293 customers Exploitable by: any authenticated customer account Developer fix: add authorization check if order.customer_id != current_user.id: return 403 Forbidden Fix deployed in 4 hours after report ✓ No customer data exposed ✓ Without pen test: attacker finds → GDPR breach + media

A6Vulnerability AssessmentsScan · Identify · Prioritize

Vulnerability assessments identify, classify, and prioritize vulnerabilities in systems without actively exploiting them. They are the systematic approach to finding technical weaknesses — the foundation of vulnerability management programs.

Pen Test vs Vulnerability Assessment

AspectVulnerability AssessmentPenetration Test
GoalFind and catalog vulnerabilitiesExploit vulnerabilities — prove impact
Exploitation?No — identify onlyYes — actively exploit
ScopeBroad — scan everythingNarrow — focused deep dive
AutomationMostly automated (Nessus/Qualys)Manual + automated — expertise required
FrequencyWeekly to monthlyAnnual or triggered
CostLow — scanner licenseHigh — skilled testers time
OutputList of vulnerabilities with CVSSExploited chain with business impact
ShowsWhat MIGHT be exploitableWhat IS exploitable with proof

Scan Types

Authenticated / Credentialed Scan

Scanner logs into each system. Sees: locally installed software, patches applied, configuration settings, registry keys, user accounts. Much more thorough — finds 4-5× more vulnerabilities than unauthenticated. Requires: service account with read access. Industry standard for internal scanning.

Unauthenticated / Passive Scan

Scanner checks only what's exposed externally. Simulates what an attacker sees without credentials. Good for: external attack surface assessment, checking for exposed services. Limited: misses internal vulnerabilities only visible from inside. Use for: externally-facing systems, periodic external perspective check.

Vulnerability Prioritization — Beyond CVSS

# CVSS score alone is insufficient for prioritization: Problem: Nessus scan finds 847 vulnerabilities Critical (9.0-10.0): 23 High (7.0-8.9): 147 Medium (4.0-6.9): 412 Low (0.1-3.9): 265 Cannot patch all at once — where to start? # Better prioritization using additional context: 1. Asset criticality: Same CVE in dev server vs production payment server → payment server gets patched FIRST 2. Exploitability in the wild (CISA KEV): CVSS 7.5 but actively exploited by ransomware groups → patch before CVSS 9.0 that's theoretical only 3. EPSS (Exploit Prediction Scoring System): Probability of exploitation in next 30 days High EPSS = patch regardless of CVSS score 4. Network exposure: Internet-facing vs internal-only Internet-facing CVE 7.0 → higher priority than internal CVE 9.0 with no external path # Patching SLA (best practice): Critical + internet-facing + KEV listed: 24-48 hours Critical: 7-14 days High: 30 days Medium: 90 days Low: 180 days or accept

CVSS →Severity score 0-10 — useful but insufficient alone for prioritization

B1Security Awareness OverviewPeople · Process · Culture

Security awareness programs educate employees to recognize and respond correctly to security threats. Technology can block most attacks — but humans remain the most exploited vector. A well-trained workforce is a critical security control.

Why People Are the Primary Target

# The human factor in breaches: 82% of breaches involve a human element (Verizon DBIR 2023) Phishing is the #1 initial access vector for most threat actors Why target humans instead of systems? Technical defenses: firewalls, EDR, patching → improving Human defenses: awareness, training, behavior → lagging A targeted phishing email: Firewall: allows it through (legitimate-looking SMTP) Email GW: delivers it (no malware signature yet) EDR: does nothing (no malware — just a link) Human: clicks link → enters credentials MFA: bypassed by real-time phishing proxy Result: full account compromise With trained employee: Same email arrives → employee recognizes indicators Employee: does NOT click, reports to security Security: blocks sender, warns others Result: attack stopped by human ✓

Components of an Effective Program

Education

Annual and ongoing security training. Policies communicated clearly. Role-specific training (finance = BEC, IT = admin attacks). Compliance training (HIPAA, PCI-DSS roles). New hire onboarding security module.

Simulation

Phishing simulations test real behavior (not just knowledge). Social engineering tests. Results: baseline + track improvement. Immediate teachable moment when employee clicks. Regular cadence — monthly or quarterly.

Culture

Security is everyone's job. Psychological safety to report mistakes. Management leads by example. Security team is approachable (not the "department of no"). Recognition for good security behaviors. Long-term behavior change — not just checkbox training.

Security culture →Psychological safety to report, management leads by example, approachable security team
B2Phishing Simulation TrainingSimulate · Measure · Educate

Phishing simulation programs send controlled fake phishing emails to employees and measure their response. They identify vulnerable individuals, provide immediate teachable moments, and track improvement over time.

Phishing Simulation Program Design

1
Establish Baseline
First simulation: no prior warning. Send moderately difficult phishing email to all staff. Measure: click rate, credential submission rate, report rate. This is your baseline — typical orgs see 20-30% initial click rate. Don't punish — data collection only. Communicate results org-wide.
2
Training Module on Click
Employee clicks link → immediately redirected to training page. "You've just been phished! Here's what to look for..." Real-time learning — mistake is fresh. Short 3-5 minute module. Covers: what indicators they missed, how to report real phishing. NOT punitive — educational. This "just-in-time training" is most effective for behavior change.
3
Increase Difficulty Over Time
Start simple → increase sophistication as employees improve. Simple: obvious phishing (Nigerian prince, spelling errors). Medium: branded email from known vendor, urgency. Hard: spear phish using employee's real manager name, relevant context. Monthly cadence with varied difficulty levels. Some employees get easier, some harder based on prior responses.
4
Track Metrics and Improve
Track: overall click rate (target <5%), report rate (target >70% of simulated phish reported), credential submission rate, department breakdown, repeat offenders. Monthly reporting to management. Annual board summary. Use data to: identify departments needing more training, adjust difficulty, identify high-risk individuals for additional intervention.
5
Address Repeat Clickers
Employees who repeatedly click despite training: additional mandatory training, manager notification, supervisor coaching. For very high-risk roles (finance handling wire transfers): more frequent simulation, additional controls (dual approval). NOT termination for first offense — training failure, not employee failure. But persistent failure with training = HR involvement.

Phishing Metrics — What to Measure

# Key KPIs for phishing simulation program: Click Rate: % of recipients who click the phishing link Baseline (untrained): 20-30% After 1 year of training: target <10% Best-in-class: <5% Credential Submission Rate: % who enter credentials on fake login page More dangerous than just clicking Target: <2% (after training) Report Rate: % of simulated phish REPORTED to security This is the real success metric Goal: employees actively defending, not just avoiding Target: >70% of employees who receive it report it Time to Report: How quickly employees report real suspicious emails Faster reporting = faster containment of real attacks Target: within 15 minutes of receiving suspicious email # Reporting cadence: Monthly: department breakdown, trend lines Quarterly: executive summary with business risk context Annual: board presentation with 12-month improvement story
// Scenario — Phishing Program Catches Real Attack
# After 12 months of phishing simulation training: Tuesday 10:23 AM — Employee in finance dept receives: From: "Microsoft Security" <[email protected]> Subject: "Urgent: Your Microsoft 365 account will be suspended" Body: "Click here to verify your account..." 18 months ago (pre-program): would have clicked Now: Employee notices: Domain: "micros0ft" — zero instead of O ← suspicious Urgency language: "urgent" + "suspended" ← pressure tactic Hover over link: goes to random IP address ← not Microsoft Employee: clicks "Report Phishing" button in Outlook Security team: receives report at 10:24 AM → Checks: is this just a simulation? No — real attack → Pulls: email from all mailboxes organization-wide → Finds: 47 employees received same email → 3 clicked: immediately reset passwords + MFA tokens → Block: sender domain on email gateway 10:47 AM: attack fully contained Training directly prevented a credential theft attack ✓ Employee who reported: positive recognition from CISO ✓

B3Phishing Attack TypesSpear · Whaling · Vishing · Smishing

Employees need to recognize all forms of social engineering attacks — not just email phishing. Training must cover every channel attackers use.

🎣
Phishing — Mass Email
Generic bulk email to many targets. "Your PayPal account has been suspended." No personalization. Low effort, high volume. Easy to spot with training. Still effective because volume ensures some clicks. Indicators: generic greeting, urgency, suspicious domain, mismatched links.
🏹
Spear Phishing — Targeted Email
Personalized email targeting specific individual or organization. Researches target: LinkedIn, company website, social media. "Hi Sarah — regarding the project with [real client name] you worked on last week..." Much harder to detect — appears legitimate. Requires more effort from attacker. Higher success rate than generic phishing. APT groups use spear phishing almost exclusively.
Spear phish example: From: [email protected] (not company.com) Subject: "Re: Q4 forecast document Sarah" "Hi Sarah, as discussed in yesterday's call, can you review the updated forecast? [Google Drive link]" Attacker researched: Sarah's name, her manager David, they had a Q4 meeting, used Google Drive → Very convincing
🐋
Whaling — C-Suite Targeting
Spear phishing targeting senior executives (CEO, CFO, CISO). High value target — executive credentials provide access to everything. Business Email Compromise (BEC): impersonates CEO to request wire transfer from finance. "I'm in a meeting, I need you to wire $150,000 to this account urgently. Don't call me — handle it." FBI reports billions lost annually to BEC.
📞
Vishing — Voice Phishing
Social engineering via phone call. "IT Support calling — we detected a virus on your computer, I need your password to fix it." Caller ID spoofing makes it appear to come from legitimate number. AI voice cloning makes it sound like known person. Helpdesk impersonation most common. Defense: verbal authentication code, call back to known number, never give password over phone.
📱
Smishing — SMS Phishing
Phishing via text message. "USPS: Your package requires delivery confirmation. Click here:" or "Your bank account has been locked — verify now." Mobile users click links more readily than on desktop. Phone number spoofing makes it appear from known sender. Short URLs on mobile make destination hard to check. Growing attack vector as mobile use increases.
💧
Watering Hole Attack
Compromise a website that the target regularly visits. Inject malicious code — all visitors get drive-by malware. Targets: industry news sites, vendor portals, forums used by target group. Harder to train against — legitimate website. Defense: web filtering, browser patching, EDR, network monitoring. Often used in APT campaigns targeting specific industries.
💾
USB Drop Attack
Leave malicious USB drives in parking lot or office. Label: "Q4 Salary Increases" or "Confidential." Employee finds it, plugs it in out of curiosity. USB auto-executes payload. Defense: policy prohibiting unknown USB devices, endpoint controls blocking unauthorized USB, security awareness training about USB attacks. Physical security control + user education required.

// Exam Keywords

Phishing →Generic mass email — low effort — still effective at volume
Spear phishing →Personalized targeted email — researched target — APT groups use this
Whaling →Targets C-suite executives — Business Email Compromise (BEC)
Vishing →Voice/phone social engineering — IT helpdesk impersonation — AI voice cloning
Smishing →SMS text phishing — USPS/bank lure — mobile users click more readily

B4Anomalous Behavior RecognitionInsider · Account · System Signals

Security awareness training teaches employees to recognize when something seems off — about their own systems, their colleagues' behavior, or organizational processes. Humans are excellent pattern matchers when taught what patterns to look for.

Employee-Recognizable Warning Signs

💻
Own System Acting Strange
Unexplained slowness, programs opening themselves, mouse moving on its own, security tools disabled, new programs installed they didn't install, files changed or missing, disk constantly active. These are indicators of active compromise or malware. Employees must know to REPORT not just reboot and hope it goes away.
Report Now
🔐
Account/Login Issues
Can't log in — password changed without their doing it. Unexpected MFA prompts. Login notifications from unknown locations. "You've logged in from Moscow" — and they're in Toronto. Emails they didn't send appearing in Sent folder. These indicate account compromise in progress. Time-critical — report immediately.
Report Now
📧
Unusual Requests
Request to bypass normal process ("don't use the ticket system — just do it"). Urgency combined with secrecy ("handle this before telling anyone"). Wire transfer request with unusual urgency (BEC). Request for credentials or access they shouldn't need. Gift card purchase request from "executive." If it feels weird — it probably is. Verify via separate channel before acting.
Verify First
👥
Colleague Behavior Changes
Coworker downloading or copying large amounts of data. Accessing systems outside their normal role. Working very late accessing systems they don't normally use. Disgruntled employee who recently received bad news (layoff, demotion). Unexplained financial improvement. These may indicate insider threat activity. Not about surveillance — about recognizing patterns that suggest harm.
Report Concern
🏢
Physical/Environmental Anomalies
Unfamiliar person in secure area without escort. Someone shoulder-surfing passwords. Unknown device connected to network port. Equipment in server room that wasn't there before. Someone taking photos of screens. These are physical security and insider threat indicators. "If you see something, say something" applies here.
Report Observed

Risky vs Policy-Violating Behaviors

B5User Guidance & Secure BehaviorsBest Practices · Policies · Hygiene

Security awareness training teaches specific behaviors that reduce risk. These are the concrete actions every employee should know and practice daily.

Password and Authentication Guidance

# What to teach employees about credentials: DO: ✓ Use unique password for every account ✓ Use a password manager (1Password, Bitwarden) ✓ Enable MFA on every account that supports it ✓ Use long passphrases over complex short passwords "correct-horse-battery-staple" > "P@$$w0rd!" ✓ Report unexpected MFA push notifications immediately DON'T: ✗ Reuse passwords across accounts ✗ Share passwords with anyone (including IT helpdesk) ✗ Write passwords on paper near computer ✗ Use personal info (birthdate, pet name) in passwords ✗ Approve MFA push you didn't initiate

Data Handling Guidance

Correct Behaviors

  • Classify data before sharing or storing
  • Use company-approved tools for work data (no personal Dropbox)
  • Encrypt sensitive data before emailing
  • Verify recipient before sending sensitive email
  • Lock screen when leaving desk (Win+L / Cmd+Ctrl+Q)
  • Use company VPN when working remotely
  • Clear desk policy — secure sensitive documents

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Emailing customer data to wrong recipient (verify twice)
  • Storing PII in personal cloud (Google Drive personal)
  • Printing sensitive data and leaving at printer
  • Disposing documents without shredding
  • Working with sensitive data in public (shoulder-surfing)
  • USB drives with unencrypted sensitive data
  • Taking photos of confidential screens

Remote Work Security Guidance

# Home/remote work security basics for all employees: Network: ✓ Use company VPN for all work activity ✓ Home WiFi: WPA3 or WPA2 + strong password ✗ Do NOT use public WiFi for sensitive work ✗ Do NOT use hotel "business center" computers Device: ✓ Use company-issued device for work ✓ Keep device updated and locked when not in use ✗ Do NOT let family members use work device ✗ Do NOT install personal apps on work device Physical: ✓ Position screen away from windows / public view ✓ Use privacy screen for sensitive work in public ✓ Lock screen when stepping away (even at home) ✗ Do NOT discuss sensitive calls in public spaces

Social Engineering Defense Checklist

🛡️
The SLAM Method for Email
Sender — Does the email address match the claimed sender? Hover to verify domain. Links — Hover over links before clicking. Does the URL match what's expected? Attachments — Are you expecting this file? Is the extension suspicious (.exe, .zip, .doc with macros)? Message — Does the message create urgency, threaten consequences, or request something unusual? If any answer is suspicious — REPORT, don't click.

Clear desk policy →No sensitive documents left on desk — lock screen when away
MFA push not initiated →Deny and report immediately — someone has your password and is trying to log in
B6Reporting Suspicious ActivityHow · Where · When

Reporting is the human sensor layer of security monitoring. Employees who report suspicious activity are the most valuable early warning system. Organizations must make reporting easy, fast, and psychologically safe.

Reporting Mechanisms

Email / Button Reporting

Phish Alert Button (KnowBe4), Report Phishing button in Outlook/Gmail. One-click reporting directly to security team. Email reports to [email protected]. Makes reporting frictionless — more reports when it's easy. Automatic analysis of reported emails.

Anonymous Reporting

Anonymous security concern hotline or web portal. Used for: insider threat concerns, witnessing policy violations, reporting colleague behavior. Employees less likely to report colleague concerns if not anonymous. Ethics hotlines handle security concerns too. Third-party hotline providers ensure anonymity.

Direct Security Contact

Clear security team contact info widely published. "If in doubt — call security immediately." Direct line / Slack channel for urgent concerns. Security team must be approachable and responsive. Bad response from security = employees stop reporting.

What to Report

# Employees must know to report ALL of these: Suspicious emails: Phishing attempts (even if they didn't click) Emails requesting unusual actions Emails from executives requesting transfers Account anomalies: Unexpected MFA notifications Password reset emails they didn't request Login alerts from unfamiliar locations Access to systems they can't remember accessing System anomalies: System acting unusual (see Tab 4) Unknown devices connected to their computer Security software disabled or alerts appearing Physical: Unknown persons in secure areas Tailgating incidents USB devices found in office areas Missing equipment or devices Policy violations observed: Colleague sending sensitive data to personal email Someone taking photos of confidential documents Shared login credentials being used # Make reporting frictionless: One-click email reporting button Security hotline number on every screen saver "Report it and let us decide if it's nothing" "No report is too small — 10 false alarms worth 1 real catch"

Psychological Safety — Making Reporting Work

# Why employees DON'T report — and how to fix it: Fear of punishment: "If I admit I clicked, I'll get fired" Fix: No blame culture — mistakes are learning opportunities Policy: clicking ≠ disciplinary action Training failure ≠ employee failure Fear of being wrong: "What if it's nothing and I look foolish?" Fix: Explicitly state: "False alarms are fine — we'd rather investigate 10 false alarms than miss 1 real attack" Positive reinforcement for reports — even false alarms Not knowing what to report: "I'm not sure if this is security-relevant" Fix: Clear examples in training — "these things = report" Rule: "When in doubt — report" Inconvenient reporting process: "I have to send an email to a random address?" Fix: One-click Phish Alert Button in email Security Slack channel for quick questions Phone number on wallpaper/screensaver When culture is right: Security team sees: suspicious email reported within 5 min Can act: pull email from all mailboxes before others click Employees ARE the security detection system ✓

// Exam Keywords

Phish Alert Button →One-click email reporting — KnowBe4 / Outlook plugin — frictionless reporting
Anonymous reporting →Hotline for insider threat concerns — employees report colleagues safely
Psychological safety →No blame culture — clicking ≠ termination — false alarms welcome
Report triggers →Phishing, account anomalies, system behavior, physical, policy violations

Awareness Program Compliance Requirements

HIPAA

Security awareness training required for all workforce members. Training at hire + when policies change. Document: who was trained, when, what was covered. Common audit ask: show us training completion records. No specific frequency mandated but "periodic" required — most do annual.

PCI-DSS

Requirement 12.6: Security awareness education at hire + annually. Include: threats and vulnerabilities, end-user responsibilities. New materials annually reflecting current threats. Evidence: training records, completion certificates, sign-off acknowledgment.

ISO 27001

Awareness of information security policy. Contribution to ISMS effectiveness. Implications of non-conformities. All personnel in scope must be aware. Evidence: training records, testing results, acknowledgments. Auditors ask employees directly: "Are you aware of the security policy?"
