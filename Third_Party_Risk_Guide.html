01Third-Party Risk OverviewTPRM · Supply Chain · Vendors

Third-party risk management (TPRM) is the process of identifying, assessing, and controlling the security risks that arise from vendors, suppliers, contractors, and other external parties that have access to your systems, data, or that influence your security posture.

Why Third-Party Risk Is So Critical

# The uncomfortable truth about third-party risk: Your security posture = YOUR controls + ALL YOUR VENDORS' controls You might have: perfect firewall, EDR on every endpoint, MFA on everything, 24/7 SOC... ...but if your IT support vendor has: shared admin password "Summer2019!" no MFA on the VPN they use to manage your systems phishing email clicked by their employee → Attacker is inside YOUR network anyway # Real breaches caused by third-party failure: Target 2013: HVAC vendor → compromised → POS malware SolarWinds 2020: Software update → 18,000 orgs compromised Kaseya 2021: MSP software → 1,500 downstream victims MOVEit 2023: File transfer software → 1000+ orgs breached Okta 2023: Support vendor → customer tenant access # Key insight: "A chain is only as strong as its weakest link" Your weakest link might be a vendor you didn't even audit

The TPRM Lifecycle

2

3

4

Vendor Classification by Risk Level

🔴
Critical / Tier 1 Vendors
Access to sensitive data (PII, PHI, financial records) OR critical systems (admin access, source code, network infrastructure). A compromise directly endangers the organization. Examples: cloud providers, MSSPs, payroll processors, core banking vendors, IT managed service providers. Highest scrutiny — full audit, on-site assessment, annual review, right-to-audit clauses.
🟠
High / Tier 2 Vendors
Limited access to sensitive data or non-critical systems. Important but not existential. Examples: marketing automation, HR software, non-core SaaS, professional services firms. Comprehensive questionnaire, SOC 2 review, annual reassessment, contract security clauses required.
🟡
Medium / Tier 3 Vendors
No access to sensitive systems or data. Indirect relationship. Examples: office supplies, facilities vendors, catering. Basic due diligence — standard questionnaire, contract review. Annual spot check. Still in scope — Target breach started with an HVAC vendor.
🟢
Low / Tier 4 Vendors
No system access, no data access, minimal business relationship. Examples: utility companies, general contractors. Basic contract only. No dedicated security assessment required. Monitor for any change in relationship that elevates risk tier.

02Vendor Assessment & Due DiligenceEvaluate Before You Trust

Vendor assessment is the process of evaluating a third party's security posture before engaging them. Due diligence means doing the work to verify — not just asking and accepting answers at face value.

Assessment Methods

📋
Security Questionnaire (Self-Assessment)
Vendor answers written questions about their security controls. Fast, low-cost, scalable. Limitation: you only know what they tell you — answers may be aspirational not actual. Must verify key claims. Used for initial screening and all tiers. Standard questionnaires: SIG (Standardized Information Gathering), CAIQ (CSA Cloud), CIS Controls questionnaire.
📄
SOC Reports — Third-Party Audit Evidence
SOC 2 Type II is the gold standard for SaaS vendors. Conducted by independent CPA firm. Covers: Security, Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, Privacy trust service criteria. Type I = design of controls at a point in time. Type II = operating effectiveness over a 6-12 month period. Always request Type II — proves controls actually WORK, not just exist.
🔍
On-Site Assessment / Audit
Your team physically visits the vendor's facility. Inspect: physical security, data center controls, employee security practices, actual system configurations. Reserved for critical (Tier 1) vendors. Requires "right to audit" clause in contract. Expensive but most thorough — you see reality not just claims.
📜
Certifications and Attestations
Independent third-party validation of vendor's security claims. ISO 27001 certificate, PCI-DSS attestation of compliance (AoC), FedRAMP authorization, HIPAA Business Associate compliance, CSA STAR certification. Reduce assessment burden — if they have ISO 27001, you can rely on that audit rather than doing your own.
🌐
Passive Monitoring / OSINT
Externally observable security indicators without vendor involvement. Security ratings services: BitSight, SecurityScorecard, UpGuard — score vendor based on observed external indicators (open ports, certificates, breach history, dark web exposure). Useful for ongoing monitoring and initial screening. Free tools: Shodan, SSL Labs, Have I Been Pwned for vendor domains.

SOC 2 Reports — Deep Dive

# SOC 2 — what to look for when reviewing: Type I vs Type II: Type I: "Controls are DESIGNED correctly" — point in time Type II: "Controls OPERATED effectively for 6-12 months" Always demand: SOC 2 Type II — Type I proves little Five Trust Service Criteria: Security: CC — Common Criteria (always included) Availability: A series (if relevant to SLA) Processing Integrity: PI series (if processing transactions) Confidentiality: C series (if handling confidential data) Privacy: P series (if handling personal data) What to review in the report: Audit period: how recent? (should be within 12 months) Auditor opinion: unqualified = clean / qualified = issues Control deviations: any controls that FAILED during period? Complementary controls: what YOU must do to make it work Scope limitations: what's EXCLUDED from the audit Red flags in a SOC 2 report: Qualified opinion → controls had failures Short audit period (3 months) → may hide issues Old report (>18 months) → controls may have changed Important controls excluded from scope Many "complementary user entity controls" → your burden

Assessment Red Flags

Immediate Disqualifiers

  • No SOC 2 or equivalent — never been audited
  • SOC 2 with qualified (failing) opinion
  • Data breach in last 12 months with no evidence of remediation
  • No dedicated security personnel
  • No security awareness training for employees
  • Customer data stored unencrypted
  • Refuses to answer questionnaire
  • Subcontracts your data to fourth parties without disclosure

Concerns Requiring Remediation Plan

  • Outdated SOC 2 (12-18 months old)
  • SOC 2 scope excludes important areas
  • Missing MFA on admin access
  • No formal incident response plan
  • Patch management SLA too long (>30 days for critical)
  • No background checks on employees with data access
  • Limited logging and monitoring capability

03Vendor SelectionEvaluate · Score · Decide

Vendor selection applies structured criteria to choose the vendor that best balances security, capability, and business value. Security must be a gate — not just a factor — for vendors with access to sensitive data or critical systems.

Selection Criteria Framework

# Weighted scoring model — security as mandatory gate: GATE CHECK (Pass/Fail — must pass all to proceed): ✓ Valid SOC 2 Type II (or equivalent) — within 12 months ✓ No unresolved critical/high findings from assessment ✓ Willing to sign DPA / BAA / NDA as required ✓ Accepts standard security contract clauses ✓ Sufficient financial stability (won't disappear) ✗ FAIL any of these → vendor rejected regardless of price/features SCORED CRITERIA (for vendors that pass the gate): Security posture: 0-25 points (weighted most heavily) Functionality: 0-20 points Price/TCO: 0-15 points SLA / reliability: 0-15 points Support quality: 0-10 points Vendor viability: 0-10 points References: 0-5 points Total: 0-100 points

The Vendor Approval Process

# Formal vendor approval workflow: Step 1 — Business Initiates Request: Business unit submits: vendor name, purpose, data access, system access, timeline, business justification Step 2 — Risk Classification: Security team assigns: tier (1-4), data classification, access level → determines assessment requirements Step 3 — Assessment: Tier 1: full questionnaire + SOC 2 review + site visit Tier 2: questionnaire + SOC 2 review Tier 3: standard questionnaire only Tier 4: basic attestation Step 4 — Security Review: Security team: reviews findings, scores, red flags Outcome: APPROVE / APPROVE WITH CONDITIONS / REJECT Step 5 — Conditional Approval: Conditions: vendor must resolve specific findings before production access granted (e.g., "enable MFA within 30 days") Step 6 — Legal / Procurement: NDA, MSA, DPA/BAA, SLA, security addendum finalized Step 7 — Onboarding: Minimum necessary access provisioned Access documented in vendor register Monitoring cadence established

Concentrating Risk — Avoiding Vendor Lock-In

Concentration Risk

Relying too heavily on a single vendor for critical functions. If that vendor has an outage, breach, or goes out of business — catastrophic impact. SolarWinds showed one vendor can impact 18,000 organizations simultaneously. Manage by: diversifying critical functions, having alternative vendors identified, limiting blast radius.

Vendor Viability

Will this vendor exist in 3 years? Startup vendors: may not survive. Source code escrow: if vendor disappears, you get source code to maintain yourself. Financial stability review: annual revenue, profitability, funding. Small vendor with no other customers = single point of failure for that vendor.

// Exam Keywords

Security as gate →Must pass security requirements first — not just a scoring factor
Vendor register →Central list of all approved vendors — access levels, assessment status, review dates
Concentration risk →Over-reliance on single vendor — SolarWinds — diversify critical functions
Vendor viability →Will vendor still exist? Financial stability + source code escrow for critical software
Minimum necessary access →Vendor gets only the access needed — not blanket access — least privilege
04Vendor AgreementsSLA · MSA · DPA · NDA

Contracts and agreements are the legal mechanism for enforcing security requirements on vendors. Without contractual obligations, a vendor has no legal obligation to meet your security standards. Security must be written into every contract — not assumed.

Key Agreement Types

SLA Service Level Agreement Defines measurable service performance commitments. Security SLAs include: uptime guarantees (99.9%), incident response times (notify within 24 hours of breach), patching windows (critical CVEs within 14 days), support response SLA, data recovery RTO/RPO. Violation = financial penalties or right to terminate. Make security SLAs specific and measurable — not vague.
MSA Master Service Agreement Overarching legal framework governing the entire relationship. Includes: security responsibilities, liability limits, indemnification, dispute resolution, intellectual property rights, security requirements the vendor must maintain throughout the relationship. Security addendum attached to MSA specifies technical requirements.
NDA Non-Disclosure Agreement Legal protection for confidential information shared with vendors. Prevents vendors from disclosing your security architecture, vulnerabilities, business plans, or customer data to competitors or the public. Mutual NDA: protects both parties. Key elements: definition of confidential info, exclusions, duration, return/destruction of info on termination.
DPA Data Processing Agreement REQUIRED by GDPR for any vendor that processes personal data on your behalf. Specifies: purpose and scope of processing, data subject rights support, security measures vendor must maintain, sub-processor disclosure and approval, data deletion on termination, audit rights, breach notification requirements (<72 hours). Without DPA = GDPR violation even if you're not doing the processing.
BAA Business Associate Agreement REQUIRED by HIPAA for vendors that access, process, or store PHI. Business Associate must agree to: protect PHI with required safeguards, report breaches within 60 days, return or destroy PHI on termination, allow HHS audit. Without BAA = HIPAA violation. Healthcare organizations must have BAA with EVERY vendor that touches PHI — including cloud providers, IT support, billing services.
MOU Memorandum of Understanding Non-binding agreement outlining a framework for cooperation. Often used between organizations before formal contracts are signed, or with government/non-profit entities. Documents intent and responsibilities but lacks legal enforcement of SLA penalties. Common in public sector and interagency agreements.
MOA Memorandum of Agreement More formal than MOU — both parties agree to specific obligations. Used when a formal contract is premature but more binding than an MOU. Defines responsibilities, resources, and timelines. Can be legally binding depending on jurisdiction and content.
OLA Operational Level Agreement Internal agreement between IT teams or departments that supports SLAs with external parties. Example: "The network team will restore connectivity within 2 hours to support the 4-hour customer-facing SLA." Aligns internal operations with external commitments. Not with vendors — between internal groups.

Critical Security Clauses in Every Vendor Contract

# Non-negotiable security clauses — require in all critical vendor contracts: 1. Security standards compliance: Vendor must maintain ISO 27001 / SOC 2 / equivalent Provide evidence annually (updated SOC 2 report) 2. Breach notification: Notify within 24-72 hours of discovering a breach Shorter than GDPR 72hr requirement — gives you time to notify Specify: what constitutes a breach, who to notify, how 3. Right to audit: Organization may audit vendor security controls Access to: facilities, systems, logs, evidence At reasonable notice (30 days) OR immediately if breach suspected 4. Data handling requirements: Data classification must be maintained Encryption standards specified (AES-256 at rest, TLS 1.3 transit) No subprocessors without prior written approval 5. Personnel security: Background checks on staff with access to your data Security awareness training — annual minimum Immediate notification if key security staff departs 6. Termination and data return: On termination: return or certifiably destroy all data Provide certificate of destruction Timeline: within 30 days of termination 7. Minimum security requirements: MFA on all admin accounts Patching SLA: critical = 14 days, high = 30 days Penetration testing: annual, results shared on request # Liability clause: Vendor is liable for breaches caused by their negligence Cap on liability: often 12 months of fees paid (negotiate higher) Data breach costs (notification, credit monitoring): vendor covers

05Security QuestionnairesSIG · CAIQ · VSQ · Custom

Security questionnaires are structured sets of questions sent to vendors to assess their security controls. They are the foundation of initial due diligence and annual reassessment. Well-designed questionnaires find real gaps — poorly designed ones just create paperwork.

Standard Questionnaire Frameworks

SIG / SIG-Lite (Shared Assessments)

Standardized Information Gathering questionnaire. Industry standard for vendor risk assessments. Full SIG: 850+ questions across 19 risk domains. SIG-Lite: abbreviated version for lower-risk vendors. Free download from Shared Assessments organization. Covers: security policies, access controls, data management, privacy, IT operations.

CAIQ (CSA Cloud Controls Matrix)

Consensus Assessments Initiative Questionnaire from Cloud Security Alliance. Specifically designed for cloud service providers. Maps to: ISO 27001, NIST CSF, PCI-DSS, HIPAA, SOC 2. Published on CSA STAR registry — vendors post answers publicly. Best for assessing cloud vendors.

Custom Questionnaires

Organization-specific questions tailored to your industry, regulatory requirements, or specific risks. Supplement standard frameworks. Include questions about: your specific compliance requirements, industry-specific threats, technology-specific controls relevant to your stack.

Sample Questionnaire — Key Domains and Questions

🔐 Domain 1 — Access Control Critical
1.1Is multi-factor authentication (MFA) required for all privileged accounts and remote access? Please describe the MFA solution used.
1.2How often are user access rights reviewed and what is the process for revoking access when an employee departs?
1.3Do you use a privileged access management (PAM) solution for admin credentials? What is the password rotation policy?
1.4How is access to our data or systems specifically scoped and documented? Can you provide a list of personnel with access?
💾 Domain 2 — Data Protection Critical
2.1What encryption standards are used for data at rest and in transit? Specify algorithms and key lengths.
2.2Where is our data physically stored? List all data center locations and cloud regions used to process/store our data.
2.3Do you engage sub-processors that may have access to our data? List all sub-processors and their locations.
2.4What is your data retention policy? How is data securely destroyed at end of retention period or contract termination?
🚨 Domain 3 — Incident Response High
3.1Do you have a documented Incident Response Plan? When was it last tested? Please summarize the plan and attach relevant documentation.
3.2What is your process for notifying customers of a security incident that affects their data? What is the target notification time?
3.3Have you experienced a security breach in the last 3 years? If yes, please describe the incident, impact, and remediation steps taken.
3.4Who is the dedicated security contact we should notify and reach out to in case of a suspected incident?
🏗️ Domain 4 — Network & Infrastructure High
4.1Is our data logically and/or physically isolated from other customers' data? Describe your multi-tenancy security architecture.
4.2What is your vulnerability management and patching SLA? How quickly are critical vulnerabilities remediated?
4.3Do you conduct regular penetration tests? By whom? How often? Can you share the executive summary of the most recent test?
4.4What security monitoring and logging tools do you use? How long are logs retained? Who has access to logs involving our data?
👥 Domain 5 — Personnel Security Medium
5.1What background checks are conducted on employees who will have access to our data or systems? How often are they renewed?
5.2Do all employees with access to our data receive security awareness training? How often? Please provide training completion rates.
5.3What is the process for managing access when an employee leaves the organization? What is the offboarding timeline?

Evaluating Questionnaire Responses

# Don't just accept answers — verify and probe: Response: "Yes, we use MFA on all admin accounts" Probe: "What MFA method? Can you show us a screenshot of the admin login screen showing MFA requirement?" Red flag: "We use SMS OTP for MFA" ← weakest MFA Response: "We have annual penetration tests" Probe: "Can you share the executive summary of the last test? What were the critical findings? How were they remediated?" Red flag: Refuses to share results ← what are they hiding? Response: "We notify customers within 24 hours of a breach" Probe: "Please provide this clause in your contract and describe the escalation path" Red flag: Not in the contract = it doesn't exist legally # Scoring questionnaire responses: Full credit: detailed answer + supporting evidence Half credit: answer given but no evidence Zero credit: vague answer / "we're working on it" / no answer Disqualifier: "no" to critical controls + no compensating control

06Ongoing Vendor MonitoringContinuous · Annual · Triggered

Vendor assessment is not a one-time event. A vendor that passed assessment two years ago may be compromised today. Continuous and periodic monitoring catches changes in a vendor's security posture before they become incidents.

Monitoring Frequency by Activity

Continuous Security ratings monitoring BitSight/SecurityScorecard alert on sudden score drops — early breach indicator. "Vendor's score dropped from 740 to 580 overnight — investigate."
Continuous Dark web / threat intel alerts Watch for vendor credentials or data appearing on dark web forums. Vendor employee credentials for sale = likely compromise in progress.
Continuous Vendor access log review Review logs of vendor access to YOUR systems. Unexpected access times, unusual queries, access outside scope = investigate immediately.
Monthly SLA compliance review Verify vendor is meeting uptime, response time, patching SLAs. Pattern of near-misses may indicate deteriorating controls before SLA breach occurs.
Monthly Security news monitoring Monitor for news about your vendors: breaches, regulatory actions, lawsuits, executive departures (especially CISO). Google Alerts on vendor names + "breach" + "security".
Quarterly Access recertification Review list of vendor personnel with access to your systems. Still employed at vendor? Still needs this access? Remove orphaned access. Critical vendors: quarterly. Others: semi-annual or annual.
Quarterly Incident and near-miss review Review any incidents involving the vendor in last quarter. Patterns of recurring issues. Trend analysis: getting better or worse? Inform next reassessment.
Annual Full reassessment Complete questionnaire cycle. Updated SOC 2 report. Verify key controls still in place. Review any changes in vendor's environment, personnel, or subprocessors.
Annual Contract review SLAs still appropriate? Security requirements still relevant? New regulations since contract signed? Renewing contract = opportunity to add stronger security requirements.

Triggered Reviews — When to Act Immediately

# Events that require immediate out-of-cycle vendor review: Trigger Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Vendor announces data breach Contact immediately — are we affected? What data? Invoke breach notification clause. Vendor CISO / security lead departs Risk of security program decline. Emergency assessment within 30 days. Meet replacement security lead. Vendor acquired by new company New ownership = different security culture. Re-assess within 60 days. New contracts may be needed. Major vulnerability in vendor Is our deployment affected? software announced What is vendor's patch timeline? (e.g., Log4Shell) Invoke patching SLA clause. Vendor subcontracts without notice Potential DPA/BAA violation. Legal review immediately. May be grounds for termination. Regulatory action against vendor Check if action affects our data. (FTC, HHS, ICO fine) Review compliance status. Reassess risk tier. Security ratings score drops 15+% Likely early sign of compromise in one week or deteriorating controls. Emergency questionnaire + contact.

Vendor Offboarding — The Critical Final Step

# Vendor relationship ends — secure offboarding checklist: Access revocation (Day 0 — contract end date): ✓ Revoke all VPN access and certificates ✓ Remove all user accounts from your systems ✓ Revoke API keys and service accounts ✓ Remove from network access control lists ✓ Revoke physical access badges (if any) ✓ Change shared credentials vendor may have known Data handling (within 30 days): ✓ Request certificate of data destruction ✓ Verify deletion of all copies including backups ✓ Recover any company IP or proprietary materials ✓ Confirm sub-processors also deleted data Documentation: ✓ Close all outstanding support tickets ✓ Document reason for termination ✓ Lessons learned — update vendor selection criteria ✓ Remove from approved vendor register ✓ Retain contract and assessment records per policy NDA survival: ✓ NDA obligations continue post-termination ✓ Vendor cannot disclose your data or architecture ✓ Document NDA survival clause in offboarding record

07Rules of EngagementPen Test · Scope · Authorization

Rules of engagement (ROE) are formal agreements that define the authorized scope, methods, timing, and boundaries for security testing activities — most commonly penetration tests and red team exercises. Without ROE, a penetration tester could face criminal charges for unauthorized computer access.

Why Rules of Engagement Matter

# Without proper ROE — legal and operational disaster: Scenario: You hire a pen testing firm No formal ROE document signed Pen tester tests "everything" — including systems they assumed were in scope Actual result: → Tests a cloud system shared with another company → Triggers their intrusion detection → calls police → Pen tester arrested under CFAA → Your company sued by the other company → Pen test achieves nothing useful # With proper ROE: Scope: EXACTLY which IP ranges, domains, systems Exclusions: EXACTLY what NOT to test (production DB, medical systems) Methods: allowed techniques (no social engineering of customers) Timing: test window (weekdays 8am-6pm only) Authorization: signed by system owner AND legal Emergency contact: if critical system disrupted — call this number Result: → Pen tester knows exactly what's authorized → Legally protected — written authorization → Business operations not disrupted → Useful findings that can be remediated

ROE Document Components

🎯
Scope Definition
Explicit list of what IS in scope: IP address ranges, domain names, application URLs, physical locations. Must be specific — "our network" is not sufficient. Include: internal IP ranges (e.g., 10.0.0.0/8), external IPs, cloud accounts (AWS account ID 123456), web apps by URL. Ambiguity = disputes or unintended testing.
🚫
Exclusions (Out of Scope)
Explicit list of what MUST NOT be tested: production payment processing during business hours, life-safety systems (hospital monitoring), systems belonging to other organizations (shared hosting), third-party SaaS services the org doesn't own, legal/compliance systems with regulatory restrictions. Tester MUST confirm exclusions before starting.
⚙️
Permitted Methods / Techniques
Which attack techniques are authorized: network scanning (yes/no), exploitation of discovered vulnerabilities (yes/no), social engineering (yes/no — and against whom), physical testing (yes/no), DoS testing (usually no — disrupts business), credential testing (yes/no), physical device implants (usually no). Each technique has different risk and different legal implications.
📅
Testing Window / Timeline
When testing is authorized: specific dates, allowed hours (9am-5pm vs 24/7), blackout periods (quarter-end, major releases, audits). After-hours testing may reduce business impact but reduces analyst availability to monitor. Time-boxed engagement: "14-day window, completing by March 31." Overtime and delays: must amend ROE.
👥
Authorization and Sign-Off
Who must sign the ROE? System owner (accountable for systems in scope), CISO (security program oversight), Legal counsel (ensure no regulatory or contractual conflicts), Executive sponsor. Without proper sign-off: tester has no legal protection. Multiple stakeholders must approve before work begins. Do NOT start until all signatures obtained.
📞
Emergency Contacts and Stop Conditions
Who to call if something goes wrong: primary contact (mobile number), secondary contact, executive on-call. Stop conditions: if testing causes unintended outage — STOP and notify immediately. If tester discovers evidence of real attacker — STOP, preserve evidence, notify. If critical system impacted — notification protocol and automatic pause.
🔒
Data Handling and Confidentiality
How findings are handled: all data captured during testing is confidential, destruction of captured data after report delivery, no publication of findings without consent, who receives the report (named individuals only), storage and transmission of the report (encrypted), retention period for evidence. Critical for organizations in regulated industries.
📋
Deliverables and Reporting
What the organization receives at end of engagement: executive summary (for non-technical leadership), technical report (detailed findings with evidence), list of vulnerabilities with severity ratings, remediation recommendations with prioritization, retest to verify remediations after fixing, debrief meeting. Format, recipient, and delivery timeline all specified in ROE.

Types of Penetration Tests — Different ROE Requirements

Black Box

Tester has NO prior knowledge of systems — simulates external attacker. Most realistic simulation. Longest time. ROE: provide only company name and target domain. No credentials, no network diagrams, no architecture docs provided to tester.

Grey Box

Tester has PARTIAL knowledge — simulates insider threat or compromised account. Provided: credentials for standard user, network diagram, some system info. More efficient than black box. ROE: specify exactly what information is provided.

White Box

Tester has FULL knowledge — source code, architecture, credentials. Most thorough — no time wasted on reconnaissance. Used for deep technical review of specific systems. ROE: full documentation provided. Tester signs strict NDA before receiving any information.

// Scenario — ROE Prevents Legal Disaster
# Pen test engagement — ROE defines what "almost went wrong": Testing day 3: Pen tester discovers: 10.0.5.0/24 has critical vulnerability CVE-2021-44228 (Log4Shell) — remote code execution Tester wants to exploit — this would prove full RCE Checks ROE: Scope: 10.0.5.0/24 is in scope ✓ Exclusions: "Do not test 10.0.5.50 — medical device" Tester: notes 10.0.5.47 is the vulnerable host 10.0.5.47 ≠ 10.0.5.50 so: cleared to exploit ✓ ROE also says: exploitation requires written approval for systems in the "critical" tag Tester contacts: primary contact (CISO) CISO: "Yes, approved — but test between 6pm-9pm only when system has no active users" Result: exploitation performed safely Full RCE demonstrated Finding: CRITICAL — in report System patched within 24 hours If no ROE / no contact process: Tester exploits 10.0.5.47 during business hours Log4Shell RCE triggers alert — SOC thinks real attack SOC isolates entire segment — takes medical systems offline Patient safety incident — legal liability Testing without ROE = real-world harm

08Supply Chain RiskSoftware · Hardware · Fourth Parties

Supply chain risk extends beyond direct vendors to the entire chain of suppliers, software dependencies, hardware manufacturers, and subcontractors that ultimately deliver a product or service. SolarWinds showed a single compromised build pipeline can affect 18,000 organizations simultaneously.

Supply Chain Attack Vectors

# How supply chain attacks work — the SolarWinds model: Normal software update: SolarWinds writes Orion update → tests → signs → publishes Your IT team: downloads update → installs → trusts it Compromised supply chain: Attackers compromise SolarWinds build environment (Dec 2019) Malicious code inserted INTO legitimate Orion update SolarWinds: signs the update (legitimate signature!) ← trusted Published: March 2020 18,000 organizations install: trust because legitimate sig Malware: activates → contacts C2 → hands over network access Discovered: December 2020 (9-MONTH DWELL TIME) # The attack was trusted because: Legitimate vendor ← trusted relationship Valid digital signature ← appears authentic Normal update process ← no reason to suspect No malware signature ← bypassed AV # Other supply chain attack types: Dependency confusion: malicious package w/ same name as private package → developers install it unknowingly Typosquatting: "reqeusts" vs "requests" Python package Compromised open source: malicious PR merged to popular library Hardware implants: hardware modified before delivery Watering hole: attack developer tools used by target's devs

Software Supply Chain Security

SBOM — Software Bill of Materials

Complete inventory of all components in a software product: open source libraries, commercial components, and their versions. Like a "nutrition label" for software. Required by US executive order for federal software. Enables: rapid response when a component has a vulnerability (Log4Shell), verification of what's actually in the software you're running.

SBOM entry example: Component: log4j-core Version: 2.14.1 License: Apache-2.0 Supplier: Apache Software Foundation CVE status: VULNERABLE (Log4Shell) → Immediate patching required

Secure Software Development

Security requirements for software you build: secure coding practices, SAST/DAST in CI/CD, dependency scanning (SCA), signed commits, code review, no hardcoded credentials. For vendors providing custom software: require these practices in the contract and verify via questionnaire and audit.

Dependency Management

Track all open source dependencies your software uses. Vulnerability scanning: Dependabot, Snyk, OWASP Dependency Check. Update strategy: pin exact versions OR auto-update with testing. Private package registry: prevents dependency confusion attacks. Never blindly install from public registries.

Code Signing

Cryptographically sign code and updates with a private key. Recipients verify signature with public key. Proves: code came from legitimate author AND wasn't modified. SolarWinds attack bypassed this because attacker compromised the signing system itself. Defense: hardware security modules (HSM) for signing keys + multi-party signing approval.

Hardware Supply Chain Risks

# Hardware can be compromised before you receive it: Risks: Counterfeit hardware: fake components with backdoors Implanted chips: malicious chip added during manufacturing Modified firmware: UEFI/BIOS modified in transit Tampered accessories: compromised USB cables, chargers Mitigations: Trusted suppliers only: approved vendor list for hardware Chain of custody: track hardware from manufacturer to deployment Tamper-evident packaging: visible if opened in transit Hardware attestation: TPM chips verify hardware integrity Firmware verification: cryptographic verification before boot Country restrictions: some orgs ban hardware from specific countries

Fourth-Party Risk

🔗
What Is Fourth-Party Risk?
Fourth parties are your vendors' vendors. You assess your vendor (third party) — but do you know who THEY rely on? Your cloud provider uses sub-processors. Your payroll vendor uses a hosting company. A breach at the fourth party can cascade to your data through your vendor. GDPR requires you to approve your vendor's sub-processors (DPA clause). Most SIG questionnaires include questions about the vendor's own vendor management program.
# Fourth-party risk chain example: Your org → Payroll vendor (third party) → Hosting company (fourth party) ↑ Hosting company breached Payroll vendor's data exposed YOUR employee data exposed # Controls for fourth-party risk: DPA clause: vendor must list all sub-processors Change notification: vendor tells you if sub-processors change Questionnaire section: "Describe your own vendor risk program" Contract clause: vendor is liable for sub-processor breaches Annual review: updated sub-processor list provided
// Scenario — Kaseya VSA Supply Chain Attack
# July 2021 — Kaseya VSA (RMM tool) supply chain attack: Context: Kaseya VSA: used by Managed Service Providers (MSPs) to remotely manage their clients' systems Attack flow: Attackers find zero-day in Kaseya VSA July 2 → deploy malicious update through VSA VSA agents: installed on MSP clients' machines Ransomware: deployed to 1,500+ companies in 22 countries Ransom demand: $70M (largest ever at the time) Why it was so effective: VSA agents: have admin access to ALL managed endpoints Legitimate software channel: not flagged as malicious Weekend attack: July 4th weekend (US) — minimal staff Scale: one vendor → many MSPs → many end customers TPRM lessons: 1. Assess your MSP's security practices — not just their capabilities 2. MSP has highest possible access — must be Tier 1 critical vendor 3. Contractual right to audit MSP security controls 4. Monitor MSP access logs continuously — detect anomalies 5. Emergency contact plan if MSP is compromised 6. Can you isolate MSP access immediately if needed? Organizations with strong TPRM: Detected anomalous behavior from VSA agents quickly Isolated MSP VPN access within hours Contained blast radius significantly
