01Incident Response ProcessNIST · PICERL · Lifecycle

Incident response is the organized approach to handling security breaches or cyberattacks. Without a documented process, responses are chaotic, slow, and inconsistent. A well-rehearsed IR plan limits damage, reduces recovery time, and preserves evidence for legal action.

What Is a Security Incident?

# Event vs Incident — key distinction: EVENT: Any observable occurrence in a system "User logged in" — "File was accessed" — "Scan detected" Millions per day — most are normal INCIDENT: Event that actually or potentially VIOLATES security policy "Unauthorized access" — "Malware detected" — "Data breach" Requires formal response # Security incident examples: ✗ Ransomware encrypting files ✗ Unauthorized access to customer database ✗ Phishing attack resulting in credential theft ✗ Insider threat exfiltrating IP ✗ DDoS attack against public-facing service ✗ Business email compromise (BEC) ✗ Supply chain compromise # Precursor vs Indicator: Precursor: sign that incident MAY occur in future (port scan = reconnaissance phase) Indicator: sign that incident IS occurring or HAS occurred (antivirus alert = active malware)

NIST SP 800-61 — The Four-Phase Model

1
Preparation
Build the team, write the plan, get the tools, run the drills. Before any incident.
2
Detection & Analysis
Identify that incident is occurring. Determine scope, severity, and nature.
3
Containment, Eradication & Recovery
Stop the bleeding, remove the threat, restore services.
4
Post-Incident Activity
Lessons learned, prevent recurrence, update defenses.

PICERL — The Six-Phase Mnemonic

Many security professionals use PICERL — a more detailed breakdown of the incident lifecycle:

PhaseFull NameKey ActivitiesDone When
P Preparation Write IRP, build CIRT, get tools, train staff, establish comms plan Ongoing — before incidents
I Identification Detect incident, verify it's real, categorize and prioritize, scope Incident suspected
C Containment Stop spread — isolate systems, block IOCs, disable accounts Incident confirmed
E Eradication Remove malware, close vulnerabilities, audit all systems Threat contained
R Recovery Restore systems, verify clean, monitor closely, return to normal ops Threat eradicated
L Lessons Learned Post-incident review, RCA, update plan, improve defenses After recovery

Incident Response Plan (IRP) — What It Contains

Roles and Responsibilities

Who is on the CIRT (Computer Incident Response Team)? Incident Commander, technical leads, legal, PR, executive sponsor. Who makes decisions? Who has authority to isolate production systems? Who approves regulatory notifications?

Communication Plan

Who gets notified at what severity level? Internal chain: SOC → CISO → CTO → CEO → Board. External: customers, regulators, law enforcement, cyber insurance. Out-of-band communications if email is compromised — use phone/Signal.

Incident Classification

Severity levels P1-P4 with definitions. P1: active ransomware, ongoing breach. P4: phishing email blocked. Each level: different response team, different escalation, different SLA for containment.

Playbooks per Scenario

Specific procedures for: ransomware, phishing, data breach, insider threat, DDoS, supply chain attack. Step-by-step actions — no need to improvise under pressure. Pre-approved response actions.

Incident Severity Classification

P1 — CRITICAL (immediate response, 24/7): Active ransomware spreading Confirmed data breach in progress Full system compromise (Domain Admin) Life-safety systems compromised Response SLA: 15 minutes P2 — HIGH (urgent, business hours + on-call): Malware detected and contained Suspected insider threat DDoS impacting services Business email compromise Response SLA: 1 hour P3 — MEDIUM (business hours): Phishing email opened (no payload executed) Suspicious but unconfirmed activity Policy violation Response SLA: 4 hours P4 — LOW (best effort): Phishing email blocked (never reached user) Automated blocked threat (no execution) Routine security event requiring review Response SLA: next business day

02IR Phases — Deep DiveContain · Eradicate · Recover

Each phase of incident response has specific actions, decisions, and considerations. Understanding what happens in each phase — and in what order — is critical for the exam and for real-world IR execution.

Phase 1 — Preparation (Before the Incident)
Everything you do before the fire starts. This is where investment matters most — reactive spending during an incident costs 3-5x more than proactive preparation.
Key preparation activities: Write and maintain Incident Response Plan (IRP) Establish Computer Incident Response Team (CIRT) Deploy detection tools: SIEM, EDR, IDS Set up communication channels (out-of-band!) Establish relationships with: law enforcement, legal, PR Obtain cyber insurance — know coverage and contacts Pre-approve IR retainer with external IR firm Secure and test backups (3-2-1 rule) Run tabletop exercises and simulations Establish forensics toolkit — hardware write blockers, forensics software, clean media Arrange out-of-band comms: Signal, phone bridge Create evidence collection procedures
Phase 2 — Detection and Analysis
Determine that an incident is occurring and understand its nature. This phase is the most cognitively demanding — you're piecing together a picture under time pressure.
Detection sources: SIEM alerts, EDR detections, IDS/IPS alerts User reports ("my files have .locked extension!") Helpdesk calls ("I can't log in") Business partner notification Third-party security researcher Dark web monitoring (your data for sale) Analysis questions — what you need to determine: WHAT type of incident? (ransomware vs APT vs insider) WHO is affected? (users, systems, data) WHERE did it start? (patient zero) HOW did attacker get in? (initial access vector) WHEN did it start? (may be weeks ago — dwell time) HOW FAR has it spread? (lateral movement scope) WHAT data was accessed/stolen/encrypted? Scope vs impact assessment: Scope: how many systems/users/data affected Impact: business damage — financial, operational, reputational Both drive: severity level and response resources
Phase 3a — Containment
Stop the bleeding. Prevent the incident from spreading further. Containment must happen BEFORE eradication — trying to clean while attacker is still active is pointless.
Short-term containment (immediate — minutes to hours): Isolate affected systems from network Block malicious IPs/domains at firewall + DNS Disable compromised user accounts Preserve evidence BEFORE making changes Capture volatile data: RAM, running processes, netstat Long-term containment (hours to days): Rebuild affected systems from clean images Apply emergency patches (virtual patching) Change all potentially compromised credentials Implement additional monitoring on remaining systems Isolate attacker while investigation continues Critical: PRESERVE EVIDENCE during containment Before isolating: capture memory dump Before reimaging: forensic disk image Before changing passwords: document current state Evidence destroyed during containment = case lost
Phase 3b — Eradication
Remove the threat completely from the environment. Eradication without complete understanding of scope risks missing persistence mechanisms.
Eradication activities: Identify ALL compromised systems (not just obvious ones) Remove malware from every affected system Delete attacker accounts and SSH keys Close initial access vulnerability (patch or config fix) Remove all persistence mechanisms: Scheduled tasks, registry run keys New admin accounts, backdoors Web shells on servers Modified legitimate files Rebuild systems that can't be fully cleaned Verify: scan all systems — confirm malware absent Update security controls to detect this attack in future
Phase 3c — Recovery
Restore systems to normal operation — carefully. Recovery is not just "turn it back on" — it requires validation that systems are truly clean before returning to production.
Recovery sequence: Restore from CLEAN backups (verify backup integrity first!) Apply all security patches before bringing online Reset ALL credentials (not just compromised ones) Test thoroughly in isolated environment before production Phased return: low-criticality systems first Intensive monitoring: watch for recurrence Validation testing: confirm no persistence remains Recovery criteria — when is it safe? All malware removed (confirmed by clean scans) Vulnerability patched (confirmed by rescan) All credentials rotated Monitoring shows no suspicious activity for 24-48 hours Management authorized return to production
Phase 4 — Post-Incident Activity (Lessons Learned)
The most valuable — and most skipped — phase. Organizations that skip lessons learned repeat incidents. This phase turns painful experience into lasting improvement.
Post-incident review meeting (within 2 weeks): Who attends: CIRT + affected business owners + management Duration: 2-4 hours (for significant incidents) Questions asked: What happened? (timeline reconstruction) How did attacker get in? (initial access vector) How long undetected? (dwell time — was it weeks?) What went well? (keep doing these things) What didn't go well? (what needs to improve) What would have stopped this? (controls to add) What would have detected this faster? Outputs: Written lessons learned report Updated IRP and playbooks Action items with owners and deadlines New security controls to implement Training gaps identified
// Scenario — Containment Order Matters
# Why you must preserve evidence BEFORE containment: Wrong order (seen in real incidents): Malware detected on server IT: "let me just reimage this server quickly" Server reimaged → malware gone → evidence gone Forensic investigator arrives: Cannot determine: how attacker got in Cannot determine: what data was accessed Cannot determine: if other systems compromised Cannot determine: was it ransomware or APT? Result: no root cause, no legal case, same attack next week Correct order: Malware detected on server Step 1: capture memory dump → evidence preserved Step 2: forensic disk image → full evidence preserved Step 3: isolate from network → stop spread Step 4: investigate → determine scope and vector Step 5: eradicate from all systems Step 6: recover from clean backups Result: root cause found, vulnerability fixed, legal case intact ✓

// Exam Keywords

Containment before eradication →Stop spread first — then remove — don't clean while attacker is still active
Preserve evidence →Capture memory dump and disk image BEFORE isolating or reimaging
Short-term containment →Immediate isolation and blocking — minutes to hours
Long-term containment →Rebuild, patch, credential rotation — hours to days
Recovery criteria →Clean scans + patch confirmed + credentials rotated + clean monitoring period
Lessons learned →Most skipped phase — turns incident into improvement — within 2 weeks
Dwell time →How long attacker was in before detection — often weeks or months
03IR Training and TestingTabletop · Simulation · Red Team

An untested IR plan is just a document. Training and testing validate that the plan works, people know their roles, and gaps are found before a real incident exposes them. The more realistic the test, the more valuable the outcome.

Training Types

Security Awareness Training

All employees — not just security team. Phishing recognition, incident reporting procedures, what to do if laptop is stolen. Empowers employees as the first line of detection. "If you see something, say something."

CIRT Technical Training

Hands-on skills for IR team members. Forensics tools, malware analysis, log analysis. Platform-specific response (Windows, Linux, cloud). Regular practice on realistic environments (ranges).

Testing Types — From Least to Most Realistic

💬 Tabletop Exercise Discussion only — zero risk

Team sits in a conference room. Facilitator presents a scenario ("It's Monday morning — you've just found out you have ransomware on 50 servers"). Team talks through their response. No systems touched. Finds: gaps in plan, communication issues, decision-making uncertainties. Cheapest and most accessible test.

What gets tested: knowledge, decision-making, communication What gets found: "Who has authority to take production down?" "We don't have the CISO's personal phone number" "Our IRP doesn't cover cloud environments" Time: 2-4 hours | Cost: $ | Risk: None | Frequency: Quarterly
🎭 Simulation Exercise Partial system involvement

Inject realistic but fake alerts into actual security tools. Team responds as if real — follows procedures, makes containment decisions, escalates. Tests: actual procedures, tools, communication. Systems touched but no real threat introduced. More realistic than tabletop.

What gets tested: actual procedures, tools usage, escalation What gets found: "Our SOAR playbook doesn't fire correctly" "We can't isolate hosts via EDR API — wrong config" "Response SLA: 4 hours — actual time: 8 hours" Time: Half-day | Cost: $$ | Risk: Low | Frequency: Semi-annual
🔴 Red Team Exercise Real attack by authorized team

Authorized attackers (red team) attempt to compromise the environment using real techniques. Blue team (defenders) responds without knowing when/how attacks come. Tests: detection capabilities, response speed, tool effectiveness. Most realistic — closest to real incident.

What gets tested: real detection + response capability What gets found: "Red team had domain admin for 3 weeks — undetected" "Our EDR was bypassed with AMSI bypass technique" "Log retention was too short — couldn't reconstruct" Time: Weeks | Cost: $$$$ | Risk: Med | Frequency: Annual
🟣 Purple Team Exercise Collaborative red + blue

Red and blue teams work TOGETHER — red team executes techniques, blue team watches and learns to detect them in real time. Highly efficient learning. Every attack technique demonstrated → blue team creates detection rule. Best for rapidly improving detection capabilities.

Purple team session example: Red: executes Pass-the-Hash technique Blue: "we can't see it — what does it look like in logs?" Red: shows the log artifacts → Blue: creates SIEM rule Test rule: Red executes again → Blue: "we see it now!" Repeat for 20 techniques in one day Result: 20 new detection rules — immediate improvement ✓
⚠️ Full Interruption Test (DR component) Highest realism — highest risk

Actually invoke DR procedures — take production systems offline and recover from backup. Tests end-to-end recovery. Highest realism. Highest risk. Usually done once per year during a planned maintenance window with management approval.

Cyber Range Training

🎯
What Is a Cyber Range?
An isolated virtual environment that replicates real infrastructure — including vulnerable systems, monitoring tools, and realistic traffic. Security teams practice attack and defense skills without risk to production. Can simulate: ransomware outbreaks, APT campaigns, insider threats. Examples: SANS NetWars, Cyber Polygon, vendor-provided ranges, internal lab environments.

04Root Cause Analysis5 Whys · Fishbone · Timeline

Root cause analysis (RCA) answers "why did this incident happen?" not just "what happened?" Without finding the true root cause, organizations patch symptoms and experience the same incident repeatedly. RCA is the bridge between incident response and lasting improvement.

Why RCA Matters

# Surface cause vs root cause — the difference matters: Incident: Ransomware encrypted 200 servers Surface cause (symptom): "Phishing email delivered ransomware" Fix: block that email sender Result: same attack via different email next month Intermediate cause: "Employee clicked link in phishing email" Fix: send employee for phishing training Result: better, but still exposed Root causes (multiple): 1. Email gateway didn't catch it (misconfigured rules) 2. No sandbox for attachment analysis 3. Employee had admin rights unnecessarily 4. EDR not deployed on affected servers 5. No MFA on admin accounts — allowed lateral movement 6. Backup monitoring not alerting on backup failures Fix: address ALL six root causes Result: ransomware can't replicate this attack pattern ✓

RCA Methods

Method 1 — The "5 Whys" Technique

Keep asking "why?" until you reach the fundamental root cause. Simple but powerful. Works for well-defined single-cause incidents.

Incident: Attacker accessed customer database Why 1: Why could attacker access the database? Because they had valid admin credentials Why 2: Why did they have valid admin credentials? Because they compromised the DBA's account Why 3: Why was the DBA's account compromised? Because DBA reused a password leaked in a breach Why 4: Why was password reuse not caught? Because no password manager policy enforced Why 5: Why was there no password manager policy? Because security policy hasn't been updated in 3 years ← ROOT CAUSE: Outdated security policy + no enforcement Fix: Update security policy + mandate password manager + enforce with technical controls (Azure AD password protection)
Method 2 — Timeline Reconstruction

Build a precise chronological timeline of all events. Reveals: how long attacker was present, what they did, which detection opportunities were missed, where controls failed.

Timeline reconstruction from logs: DAY -47 (6 weeks before detection): 09:14 — Phishing email received (Email GW: ALLOWED) 09:17 — Employee clicked link (Proxy log: recorded) 09:18 — Malware downloaded (EDR: NOT deployed on this host) 09:19 — Initial C2 connection established DAY -35: 14:22 — Lateral movement: PsExec to file server 14:25 — Credential harvest: Mimikatz executed (SIEM: no alert) DAY -14: 02:11 — Domain Admin credentials obtained 02:15 — 200 servers enumerated DAY 0 (discovery): 07:45 — Ransomware deployed to all 200 servers 07:47 — EDR (on workstations): finally detected Dwell time: 47 days undetected RCA: EDR gap + SIEM rule gap + email gateway gap
Method 3 — Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram

Visual cause-and-effect analysis. The "head" is the problem. "Bones" represent cause categories. Good for complex incidents with multiple contributing factors across different domains.

PEOPLE PROCESS TECHNOLOGY │ │ │ No security ─┤ No patch ─────┤ EDR gap ───────┤ awareness │ process │ │ │ │ No MFA ────────┤ Shared admin─┤ No change ────┤ │ passwords │ management │ Flat network───┤ │ │ │ └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘ │ RANSOMWARE INCIDENT

RCA Report — What It Contains

Report Contents

  • Executive summary (1 page — no jargon)
  • Full incident timeline
  • Technical details of attack chain
  • Root cause(s) identified
  • Contributing factors
  • Detection failures and gaps
  • Response analysis (what worked/didn't)
  • Remediation actions — specific, owned, dated
  • Recommendations to prevent recurrence

Common RCA Findings

  • Missing security controls (EDR not deployed)
  • Misconfigured controls (email gateway rules)
  • Unpatched vulnerabilities (known CVE exploited)
  • Excessive privileges (least privilege not applied)
  • Missing MFA on critical accounts
  • Inadequate logging (couldn't reconstruct timeline)
  • Outdated/untested backups
  • Lack of network segmentation

05Threat HuntingProactive · Hypothesis · IOC

Threat hunting is proactive — humans actively searching for threats that have evaded automated detection. It's the antidote to long dwell times. Rather than waiting for alerts, hunters assume attackers are already inside and go looking for them.

Reactive vs Proactive Security

# Reactive security (alert-driven): Wait for SIEM/EDR to alert Investigate the alert Respond to confirmed threat Problem: alerts only fire for KNOWN attack patterns Advanced attackers evade detection intentionally Average dwell time: 207 days before alert fires # Proactive threat hunting: Assume: "Attacker is already inside — where are they?" Form hypothesis: "What would living-off-the-land look like?" Hunt: search data for evidence of that technique Find: threats that evaded all automated tools Result: attacker found in hours/days vs months Key principle: "Assume breach" mentality

Threat Hunting Process

Step 1 — Form a Hypothesis

Based on: threat intelligence (new TTPs reported), industry alerts (CISA advisories), gut feeling from experienced hunters, previous incident patterns. "I think an attacker might be using [specific technique]. Let me look for evidence of it."

Hypothesis examples: "An attacker is using WMI for lateral movement" "Malware is using DNS tunneling for C2" "Credentials are being harvested via LSASS dumping" "An insider is staging data for exfiltration" "An APT is using living-off-the-land techniques"
Step 2 — Collect and Analyze Data

Query SIEM, EDR, and network data for evidence related to hypothesis. Look for: anomalies from baseline, patterns matching the hypothesized technique, artifacts in logs.

# Hunting for WMI-based lateral movement: Splunk query: index=endpoint source_type=win:edr process_name="wmiprvse.exe" | where parent_process_name NOT IN ("svchost.exe","services.exe") | stats count by host, user, cmdline # Results: wmiprvse.exe spawned by powershell.exe # on 3 servers — suspicious! investigate further
Step 3 — Investigate Leads

Dig into anomalies found. Correlate with other data sources. Timeline the activity. Determine if benign or malicious. Context matters — same technique can be admin or attacker.

Step 4 — Act on Findings

If threat confirmed: initiate incident response. Either way: document findings, create detection rules based on the hunt, share IOCs with threat intel platform. Every hunt improves future detection capabilities.

Hunt outcome 1 — Threat found: Attacker discovered using WMI lateral movement IR triggered: active incident Attacker found BEFORE ransomware deployed ✓ Hunt outcome 2 — No threat found: No evidence of WMI lateral movement Document: baseline established Create: detection rule for future Result: improved detection coverage ✓

IOCs vs TTPs — What Hunters Use

IOCs — Indicators of Compromise

Specific artifacts of known attacks. Easy to detect but easy for attackers to change. Short shelf life — attackers rotate IPs, domains, hashes constantly.

  • IP addresses of C2 servers
  • Malware file hashes (MD5/SHA256)
  • Malicious domain names
  • Registry keys created by malware
  • File paths of dropped malware
  • Specific strings in network traffic

IOC hunting = looking for yesterday's attack

TTPs — Tactics, Techniques, Procedures

HOW attackers behave — harder to change than IOCs. Even if attacker changes tools, TTPs remain consistent. MITRE ATT&CK framework catalogs TTPs. TTP hunting finds advanced attackers who rotate IOCs.

  • Spearphishing with Office macro (T1566)
  • LSASS credential dumping (T1003)
  • Living-off-the-land binaries (T1218)
  • Kerberoasting (T1558.003)
  • Scheduled task persistence (T1053)
  • C2 over DNS (T1071.004)

TTP hunting = finding today's attack regardless of tools

MITRE ATT&CK Framework

# MITRE ATT&CK — organized catalog of attacker TTPs 14 Tactics (high-level goals): Initial Access → Execution → Persistence → Privilege Escalation → Defense Evasion → Credential Access → Discovery → Lateral Movement → Collection → C2 → Exfiltration → Impact Each Tactic has Techniques: Credential Access: T1003 — OS Credential Dumping T1003.001 — LSASS Memory T1003.002 — SAM T1003.004 — LSA Secrets # Threat hunters use ATT&CK to: 1. Identify which techniques to hunt for 2. Map existing detections to ATT&CK → find gaps 3. After incident: map attack chain to techniques 4. Prioritize detection development 5. Communicate with other defenders ("T1059.001") Example hunt hypothesis from ATT&CK: "Hunt for T1003.001 — LSASS dumping" Known artifacts: procdump.exe targeting lsass.exe Mimikatz process or strings comsvcs.dll MiniDump Task Manager creating lsass.dmp
// Scenario — Threat Hunt Finds APT
# Threat hunter hypothesis: "Is any process beaconing to C2?" # Beaconing = regular connection intervals = malware C2 Hunter queries NetFlow data: Find: outbound connections with regular intervals Results after analysis: Host: 10.1.4.22 (WORKSTATION-47) Destination: 185.220.101.89 Pattern: connection every 5 minutes ±30 seconds Started: 43 days ago This looks like beaconing! Investigate further: VirusTotal: 185.220.101.89 → known APT C2 EDR: process making connections = svchost.exe BUT: running from C:\Users\user\AppData (unusual path) Memory: injected shellcode in svchost APT discovered — 43-day dwell time Never triggered any automated alert Response: IR initiated immediately Forensic image captured Host isolated Full scope investigation: 12 other hosts also infected All cleaned before attacker achieved their final objective Threat hunt: found what 43 days of automated detection missed ✓

06Digital ForensicsAcquire · Preserve · Analyze

Digital forensics is the science of collecting, preserving, and analyzing digital evidence in a way that maintains its integrity for legal proceedings. Done incorrectly, evidence is inadmissible in court and legally worthless.

Forensics Fundamentals

# Core forensics principles: 1. Preserve first, analyze second: Create forensic copy (image) BEFORE anything else Analyze the COPY — never the original Original evidence: untouched, chain of custody maintained 2. Maintain chain of custody: Document: who had evidence, when, what they did Gaps in chain = evidence inadmissible Form signed at every transfer 3. Document everything: Hash before and after — prove nothing changed Photograph the scene before touching anything Notes: time, date, location, conditions, who present 4. Order of volatility: Collect most volatile evidence FIRST Most volatile → least volatile: CPU registers, cache → RAM → swap/page file → Running processes → network connections → File system → magnetic media → optical media 5. Don't contaminate: Use write blockers — prevent any writes to original media Clean media for storage — verified empty Forensic workstation — no internet, clean OS

Order of Volatility — Critical for Exam

# Collect in this order — most volatile first: 1. CPU registers and cache (lost when process ends) 2. RAM / system memory (lost when power off) 3. Swap file / page file (may be overwritten) 4. Running processes (end when system reboots) 5. Network connections (close when system reboots) 6. Network routing tables, ARP (lost on reboot) 7. File system / disk (persists — but files can be deleted) 8. Removable media (persists — unless destroyed) 9. Remote logging / SIEM (most persistent — log retention policy) 10. Printed records (physical — most permanent) # Why order matters: If you reboot first: lose RAM, processes, connections If you image disk first: RAM already changing — process using memory Collect RAM → then network state → then disk image

Types of Forensic Evidence

💾 Disk Forensics Most Common

Forensic image of entire disk (bit-for-bit copy). Includes deleted files, slack space, unallocated clusters. Tools: FTK Imager, dd (Linux), Autopsy. Analyze: file system artifacts, browser history, registry, event logs, deleted files, file metadata (timestamps).

# Create forensic image with write blocker: Write blocker → evidence drive → forensic workstation FTK Imager: File → Create Disk Image Source: physical drive (via write blocker) Destination: forensic image (.E01 format) Hash: SHA-256 calculated during acquisition Result: exact copy — SHA-256 hash documented Original: untouched — goes into evidence bag Analysis: performed on copy only
🧠 Memory Forensics Catches Fileless Malware

Analyze RAM contents. Finds: running processes, network connections, encryption keys, fileless malware, injected code, decrypted data that was encrypted on disk. Tool: Volatility (open source, most widely used). Must capture BEFORE powering off.

# Volatility memory analysis commands: vol.py -f memory.dmp imageinfo # identify OS vol.py -f memory.dmp pslist # running processes vol.py -f memory.dmp netscan # network connections vol.py -f memory.dmp cmdline # process command lines vol.py -f memory.dmp malfind # injected code vol.py -f memory.dmp dumpfiles -r evil # extract files # Memory finds what disk analysis misses: Fileless malware: visible in RAM ✓ Encryption keys: decrypted in RAM ✓ Packed malware: unpacked in RAM ✓
📡 Network Forensics Traffic Analysis

Analyze captured network traffic (PCAP). Reconstruct: what data was exfiltrated, C2 communications, lateral movement, initial access method. Tools: Wireshark, NetworkMiner, tcpdump, Zeek/Bro.

# Wireshark filters for incident investigation: ip.addr == 185.220.101.34 # filter C2 IP http.request.method == "POST" # data being sent out dns.qry.name contains "evil" # suspicious DNS tcp.flags.syn == 1 # new connections frame.len > 1000 and tcp.dstport == 53 # DNS tunneling?
📱 Mobile Device Forensics iOS / Android

Specialized area — mobile devices have different acquisition methods. Logical extraction (like backup), filesystem extraction, physical extraction. Challenges: encryption, MDM management, biometric locks. Tools: Cellebrite, Oxygen Forensics.

☁️ Cloud Forensics Emerging Area

Traditional forensics methods don't directly apply to cloud. Evidence: API logs (CloudTrail), object storage logs, IAM activity logs, VPC flow logs. Challenge: shared infrastructure, limited access, data in multiple jurisdictions, provider cooperation needed.

# AWS forensic evidence sources: CloudTrail: all API calls — who did what VPC Flow Logs: network traffic metadata S3 access logs: object-level access GuardDuty: threat detection findings Config: configuration change history CloudWatch: application and system logs

07Evidence Handling & Chain of CustodyLegal · Integrity · Admissibility

Evidence that is improperly collected, handled, or documented cannot be used in court. Chain of custody is the documented record that proves evidence was not tampered with between collection and courtroom.

Chain of Custody

# Chain of custody = who had evidence, when, what they did Every transfer documented: Collected by: Jane Smith, Forensic Analyst Date/Time: 2024-03-15 14:32:17 UTC Location: Server Room B, Server: DB-PROD-01 Evidence: 2x 4TB Samsung SSDs Hash (SHA-256): Disk1: a3f8b2c9d1e4... Disk2: 7k2m9p4q8r1... Sealed: Tamper-evident bag #FE-2024-0847 Transferred to: Mark Johnson, Lead Investigator Date/Time: 2024-03-15 16:15:00 UTC Reason: forensic analysis Seal intact: YES ✓ Signature: M. Johnson In evidence storage: Storage: Locked cabinet, Evidence Room Access log: maintained Transferred to: Lab for analysis Date/Time: 2024-03-16 09:00:00 UTC Seal verified intact before opening: YES ✓ Unbroken chain — evidence admissible ✓

Hashing for Evidence Integrity

# Hash proves evidence wasn't modified — mathematical fingerprint Step 1 — Hash at collection: sha256sum evidence_drive_001.img Output: a3f8b2c9d1e4f5a6b7c8d9e0f1a2b3c4 evidence_drive_001.img Document: write on evidence form, photograph Step 2 — Hash before analysis: sha256sum evidence_drive_001.img Output: a3f8b2c9d1e4f5a6b7c8d9e0f1a2b3c4 ← MATCHES ✓ If changed: evidence tampered — inadmissible Step 3 — Hash after analysis: sha256sum evidence_drive_001.img Output: a3f8b2c9d1e4f5a6b7c8d9e0f1a2b3c4 ← MATCHES ✓ Analysis did not modify evidence: confirmed # Hash algorithm choice: MD5: 128-bit — fast — collision vulnerabilities found Still used for integrity (not cryptographic purposes) SHA-1: 160-bit — deprecated — collisions possible SHA-256: 256-bit — current standard — use this SHA-3: newest — highest security — slowly adopted

Evidence Collection Best Practices

01 Photograph the scene Before touching anything — document original state of every connected cable, screen state, visible files. Time-stamped photos from multiple angles.
02 Capture volatile data first Memory dump, running processes, network connections — before any other action. WinPmem, DumpIt, or LiME for Linux RAM capture.
03 Use write blockers Hardware write blocker between evidence drive and forensic workstation. Prevents any writes to original. Every forensic lab has these. Never image without one.
04 Hash everything Calculate SHA-256 of every piece of evidence at collection and at every step. Document hashes on chain of custody form.
05 Seal in tamper-evident packaging Tamper-evident bags with unique serial numbers. Any breach visible. Maintain in locked, access-controlled evidence storage.
06 Label everything Every item: case number, date, time, collector name, description, location found. Labels that can't be removed or altered.
07 Analyze copy — never original Original evidence: stays sealed and untouched. All analysis on forensic copy. If copy corrupted or lost — can make another from original.

Legal Holds and e-Discovery

Legal Hold

When litigation is anticipated, normal retention policies are SUSPENDED. All relevant data must be preserved exactly as-is. Deleting data under legal hold = obstruction of justice. IT must immediately halt: auto-delete policies, log rotation, email purging for relevant accounts.

e-Discovery

Electronic discovery — process of identifying, collecting, and producing electronically stored information (ESI) for legal proceedings. Forensic tools used to search, collect, and produce evidence. Tools: Relativity, Nuix, Cellebrite. Must preserve metadata — modification timestamps are evidence.

External Notification Requirements

Regulatory Notifications

Many regulations require breach notification within specific time windows:

  • GDPR: 72 hours to supervisory authority
  • HIPAA: 60 days to HHS; < 60 days to patients
  • PCI-DSS: Immediately to card brands and acquirer
  • SEC (public co): 4 business days (material)
  • State laws: Vary — some as quick as 30 days

Missing notification deadlines = additional regulatory fines

Customer Notification

If customer data compromised: legal obligation to notify. Notification must include: what data was exposed, when, what you're doing about it, what customers should do. Tone: factual, clear, apologetic without admitting legal liability. Legal review required before sending.

Law Enforcement

When to involve: financial loss, potential for prosecution, nation-state involvement. FBI (US) — IC3 for cybercrime. CISA for critical infrastructure attacks. Pros: access to threat intel, legal authority. Cons: loss of control over investigation timeline.

Cyber Insurance

Notify insurer IMMEDIATELY — most policies require prompt notification. Failure to notify = claim denied. Insurer: provides IR resources, legal counsel, PR firm. Read policy before incident — understand coverage and requirements.

Media and PR During an Incident

Result: $148M settlement + criminal charges for CSO Bad: Equifax 2017: Executives sold stock between breach and disclosure Disclosure delayed for 6 weeks Website set up to check status had vulnerabilities Result: congressional hearings + massive fine Best practices for public communication: Be transparent — disclose what you know, when you know it Don't speculate — only confirmed facts in statements One spokesperson — no conflicting messages from multiple execs Pre-approved statement templates — ready BEFORE incident "We are investigating and will provide updates" is fine initially Update regularly — silence breeds rumor Never say "unhackable" — you'll regret it Legal reviews all external statements
