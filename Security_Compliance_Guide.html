01Compliance OverviewRules · Obligations · Accountability

Security compliance means meeting the security obligations imposed by laws, regulations, contracts, and standards. It answers: What are we required to do? Are we actually doing it? How do we prove it? What happens if we don't?

Why Compliance Is More Than a Checkbox

# Two ways to think about compliance: Compliance as checkbox (wrong): "We just need to pass the audit" "Do the minimum to avoid fines" "Security exists to satisfy regulators" Result: Technically compliant but actually insecure Passes audit → gets breached two months later Compliance theater — appearance without substance Compliance as foundation (right): Regulations encode minimum security baselines Compliance floor → build security culture on top "We follow HIPAA because our patients trust us" "PCI-DSS protects our customers' payment data" Result: Security and compliance aligned → both improve together Regulators see genuine commitment → lighter treatment Customers trust you → competitive advantage # Reality: Compliance ≠ security (you can be compliant AND breached) Security ≠ compliance (you can be secure AND non-compliant) Goal: be BOTH — compliant AND genuinely secure

Compliance Sources — Where Obligations Come From

Laws & Regulations

Government mandates. GDPR, HIPAA, SOX, CCPA. Legal obligation. Non-compliance: fines, criminal charges, license revocation. No opt-out.

Industry Standards

Sector-specific requirements. PCI-DSS (payments), NERC CIP (energy), CMMC (defense). Contractual requirement to participate in industry. Violation: lose ability to operate in sector.

Contracts

Customer and partner requirements. SOC 2 often required by enterprise customers. ISO 27001 required in some supply chains. Violation: contract termination, civil suit.

Voluntary Frameworks

Best practices adopted by choice. NIST CSF, CIS Controls. No legal mandate but improve security. Often used as evidence of good faith in breach litigation.

Compliance vs Risk Management — Working Together

# Compliance sets the FLOOR — risk management goes higher: HIPAA requires: encrypt PHI in transit Risk assessment: finds: PHI also stored unencrypted on employee laptops (not a HIPAA requirement) Risk treatment: implement laptop encryption anyway Result: HIPAA compliant AND more secure than required # Compliance gap analysis process: 1. What does the regulation require? 2. What do we currently have? 3. Gap: what's missing or insufficient? 4. Remediation plan: close the gap 5. Evidence collection: prove controls work 6. Reporting: demonstrate compliance to auditors # Same control can satisfy multiple requirements: Encryption at rest → satisfies: HIPAA + PCI-DSS + GDPR + SOX MFA on admin accounts → satisfies: NIST 800-53 + ISO 27001 + PCI Audit logging → satisfies: HIPAA + SOX + PCI-DSS + NIST

02Key Regulations & StandardsGDPR · HIPAA · PCI · SOX · More

These are the regulations most commonly tested on Security+. Know what each protects, who must comply, key requirements, and notification deadlines.

GDPR General Data Protection Regulation
EU regulation protecting personal data of EU residents. Applies globally — any organization that processes EU resident data is in scope regardless of where the org is based. Landmark privacy regulation — widely copied by other jurisdictions.
Scope: Any org processing EU resident personal data Enforcement body: National Data Protection Authorities (DPA) Key principles: Lawful basis required, data minimization, purpose limitation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity and confidentiality, accountability Breach notification: 72 hours to supervisory authority Without undue delay to data subjects if high risk Max fines: €20 million OR 4% of global annual turnover (whichever is HIGHER) Example fine: Amazon: €746M / Meta: €1.2B / Google: €50M Data subject rights: Access, Rectification, Erasure (right to be forgotten), Portability, Object, Restriction DPO required when: Public authority / large-scale processing / special category data processing at scale
72-HR NOTIFICATION 4% REVENUE FINE RIGHT TO ERASURE DPO REQUIRED
HIPAA Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act
US federal law protecting Protected Health Information (PHI). Applies to covered entities (healthcare providers, health plans, clearinghouses) AND their business associates. Three key rules.
Three rules: Privacy Rule: Who can access/use PHI, patient rights Security Rule: Administrative, physical, technical safeguards for electronic PHI (ePHI) Breach Notification Rule: Report breaches of unsecured PHI PHI definition: Any info linking patient + health condition + healthcare/payment (name, SSN, DOB, address + health info) Breach notification: Individuals: within 60 days of discovery HHS: within 60 days Media: if >500 in a state → notify media HHS Wall: breaches <500 reported annually Minimum necessary: Access only the PHI needed for the purpose Business Associates: must have signed BAA — also must comply Penalties: Tiered: $100–$50,000 per violation Max $1.9M per violation category per year
60-DAY NOTIFICATION BAA REQUIRED MINIMUM NECESSARY $1.9M/YR MAX
PCI-DSS Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard
NOT a law — a contractual standard from major card brands (Visa, Mastercard, AmEx, Discover, JCB). Required for any organization that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data. 12 requirements organized around 6 goals.
Applies to: Any org handling credit/debit card data Enforced by: Card brands + acquiring banks (not government) Violation: Lose ability to process cards + fines from card brands 12 Requirements (6 goals): Build secure network: Firewall, no vendor defaults Protect cardholder: Protect stored data, encrypt in transit Vulnerability mgmt: AV software, secure systems Access control: Need-to-know, unique IDs, physical Monitor/test: Track access, test security systems Policy: Security policy Scanning requirements: Quarterly: External vulnerability scans by ASV Annual: Penetration test Merchant levels: Level 1: >6M transactions/year → on-site audit (QSA) Level 2-4: lower volume → SAQ (Self Assessment Questionnaire)
NOT A LAW QUARTERLY ASV SCANS 12 REQUIREMENTS CARD PROCESSING REVOKED
SOX Sarbanes-Oxley Act
US federal law for public companies focused on financial reporting integrity. Requires CEOs and CFOs to personally certify financial reports. IT security directly relevant because financial data lives in IT systems — IT general controls are audited as part of SOX compliance.
Applies to: US publicly traded companies + their subsidiaries Key sections: Section 302: CEO/CFO certify financial statements Section 404: Management + auditor assess internal controls Section 802: Document retention — fines for destruction IT relevance: Access controls on financial systems → who can modify data? Change management → unauthorized changes to financial systems? Audit logs → 7-year retention required Separation of duties → developer ≠ production deployer Penalties: Criminal: up to 20 years imprisonment for execs Civil: fines for non-compliant orgs
CEO/CFO CERTIFY 7-YR LOG RETENTION 20-YR CRIMINAL SECTION 404 AUDIT
CCPA California Consumer Privacy Act
California state law giving California residents rights over their personal data. Often called "America's GDPR." Applies to for-profit businesses meeting certain thresholds. Amended by CPRA (California Privacy Rights Act) in 2023.
Applies to: For-profit orgs doing business in CA AND: >$25M revenue OR process data of >100K Californians OR >50% revenue from selling personal data Consumer rights: Right to know: what data is collected and shared Right to delete: request deletion of personal data Right to opt-out: stop sale of personal data Right to non-discrimination: can't penalize for exercising rights Right to correct: fix inaccurate personal data (CPRA addition) Fines: $2,500 per unintentional violation $7,500 per intentional violation Private right of action for data breaches: $100-750/consumer
OPT-OUT RIGHT CALIFORNIA RESIDENTS $7,500 INTENTIONAL

03Compliance ReportingInternal · External · Attestation

Compliance reporting is the formal process of documenting and communicating compliance status to regulators, auditors, executives, and customers. Without evidence, compliance doesn't exist in the eyes of an auditor.

Internal Compliance Reporting

# Internal compliance reporting — keeping leadership informed: Board / Audit Committee (quarterly/annual): Compliance posture summary — are we meeting obligations? Open regulatory findings and remediation status Upcoming compliance deadlines Resource needs for compliance activities Significant compliance incidents (near-misses, violations) Benchmarking vs peers CISO / Executive Team (monthly): Compliance dashboard — control status by regulation New regulatory developments affecting us Assessment results and findings Policy exceptions outstanding Training completion rates Vendor compliance status Security team / Compliance team (weekly/ongoing): Control testing results Evidence collection status Audit preparation progress Policy exceptions queue Vulnerability scan compliance metrics

External Compliance Reporting

Regulatory Reporting

Mandatory reports to government agencies. HIPAA: report breaches to HHS, post on Wall of Shame if >500. GDPR: notify Data Protection Authority. SEC: material cybersecurity incidents within 4 business days. FISMA: annual reporting to OMB/CISA. These are legal obligations with deadlines — missing them = additional violation.

Customer and Partner Reporting

SOC 2 Type II report shared with customers annually. ISO 27001 certificate as evidence of ISMS. PCI-DSS Attestation of Compliance (AoC) shared with acquiring bank. Contract may require: annual security assessment, penetration test summary, security awareness training completion rates. Builds customer trust.

Key Compliance Report Types

📋
SOC 2 Report
System and Organization Controls report from independent CPA. Covers Trust Service Criteria. Shared with customers under NDA as proof of security controls. Type II (operating effectiveness) is the gold standard. Annual re-audit required to maintain. Organizations without a SOC 2 struggle to win enterprise customers.
📜
Attestation of Compliance (AoC) — PCI-DSS
Formal declaration by the organization and their QSA (Qualified Security Assessor) that PCI-DSS requirements were met for the assessed period. Submitted to acquiring bank. Different from the full Report on Compliance (ROC) — the AoC is the summary declaration, the ROC is the detailed evidence report.
📊
HIPAA Risk Assessment Report
HIPAA Security Rule requires covered entities to conduct and document an annual risk assessment. Must identify ePHI, threats, vulnerabilities, controls, likelihood, impact, and risk level. Documented risk analysis is itself required — not just doing the analysis. HHS audits look for this documentation first. Most commonly cited violation: no documented risk analysis.
🏛️
Breach Notification Reports
Formal reports to regulators and affected individuals when a breach occurs. GDPR: formal notice to DPA within 72 hours, then detailed follow-up within 30 days. HIPAA: HHS OCR web portal submission. SEC: 8-K filing within 4 business days for material incidents. Notification letters to affected individuals — often reviewed by legal before sending. Strict templates in some jurisdictions.

Evidence Collection — The Foundation of Compliance Reporting

# Auditors need EVIDENCE — not assertions: "We have MFA enabled" → Show: screenshot of MFA policy + report showing 100% enrollment rate "We patch within 14 days" → Show: patch compliance report showing critical vulns remediated with dates "Employees are trained" → Show: LMS report — 100% completion + training content covered + signatures "Access is reviewed" → Show: quarterly access review reports + evidence of revocations actioned "We log all access" → Show: SIEM configuration + sample log retention report + log integrity check # Evidence best practices: Collect continuously — not just before audit Use a GRC tool to track evidence to controls to requirements Timestamp and version-control evidence Store in audit-ready repository — auditor gets read access Evidence for every control — gaps = findings = remediation work

GRC Tools — Automating Compliance

What GRC Tools Do

GRC (Governance, Risk, and Compliance) platforms: map controls to multiple frameworks simultaneously (HIPAA + PCI + ISO 27001), track evidence collection, manage policy lifecycle, automate compliance reporting, maintain risk register, track audit findings. Examples: ServiceNow GRC, Archer, OneTrust, Drata, Vanta.

Automated Evidence Collection

Modern compliance platforms integrate with: cloud environments (AWS/Azure), endpoint management (MDM), identity systems (Azure AD), SIEM, vulnerability scanners. Automatically pull evidence of control operation. MFA enabled? GRC pulls from Azure AD. Patching current? GRC reads from WSUS/Qualys. Reduces manual effort dramatically.

Audit Management

When auditors arrive: GRC generates pre-packaged evidence for each requirement. Auditor gets read-only portal access. All findings tracked and remediated within the system. Audit trail of who collected what evidence when. Re-audit preparation built in — track remediation of prior findings.

04Consequences of Non-ComplianceFines · Criminal · Business Impact

Non-compliance consequences extend far beyond fines. The full spectrum of consequences — financial, legal, operational, and reputational — often dwarfs the cost of compliance itself.

Financial Fines — Real Numbers

GDPR Up to €20M or 4% revenue Meta: €1.2B (2023) / Amazon: €746M (2021) / WhatsApp: €225M — whichever is higher
HIPAA $100–$50,000 per violation Max $1.9M per category/yr. Anthem: $16M / Premera: $6.85M / UCLA Health: $865K
PCI-DSS $5,000–$100,000/month From card brands to acquiring bank, passed to merchant. Plus card replacement costs, forensic audit costs, increased transaction fees.
CCPA $2,500–$7,500 per violation Per consumer per violation — multiplies rapidly. Sephora: $1.2M settlement. Private right of action: $100-$750 per consumer for data breaches.
FTC (US) Up to $50,120/day per violation Facebook/Meta: $5B (2019 Cambridge Analytica). Equifax: $575M FTC settlement. Can mandate specific security improvements.

Full Spectrum of Consequences

💰
Financial Penalties
Regulatory fines are just the beginning. Add: legal defense costs (millions per case), breach notification costs ($50-100/affected individual for credit monitoring), forensic investigation fees, IT remediation costs, increased cyber insurance premiums (often 2-5× after a breach), card replacement costs (PCI breaches). Total cost of a major breach: often 10-100× the regulatory fine alone.
⚖️
Legal and Criminal Liability
SOX: executives can face up to 20 years imprisonment for knowingly certifying false financial reports. HIPAA: criminal charges — $250K fine + 10 years for "wrongful disclosure." Civil lawsuits from affected individuals (class action). FTC enforcement actions with consent decrees requiring 20 years of monitoring. Directors and Officers (D&O) personal liability for negligent governance.
🚫
Loss of Business Privileges
PCI-DSS non-compliance: card brands revoke ability to process credit/debit cards — business cannot accept card payments. Healthcare: CMS can exclude provider from Medicare/Medicaid participation. Federal contracts: loss of security clearance or government contract eligibility. Operating licenses can be revoked by state regulators in financial and healthcare sectors.
📉
Reputational Damage
Breaches become public. HHS "Wall of Shame" (HIPAA breaches >500). SEC mandatory disclosures for material incidents. Press coverage and social media amplification. Stock price impact: 3-5% average drop on disclosure. Long-term customer churn — especially B2B enterprises that remove non-compliant vendors from supply chain. Brand recovery takes years.
📏
Regulatory Oversight and Remediation Orders
Beyond fines: regulators impose Consent Decrees requiring specific improvements, monitored by independent assessors, lasting 20+ years (FTC standard). Corrective Action Plans (CAP) with mandatory timelines. Mandatory third-party security audits at organization's expense. Some regulators (GDPR, HIPAA) can conduct unannounced inspections. Ongoing oversight is often more burdensome than the initial fine.
🏗️
Operational Disruption
Regulator-mandated system shutdowns (HIPAA: HHS can require ePHI system shutdown). Incident response and forensic investigation while trying to run the business. Leadership distraction — breach response consumes CISO, CEO, General Counsel time for months. System remediations that interrupt normal operations. Court-ordered discovery consumes IT and legal resources.

Due Care vs Due Diligence

Due Diligence

Doing the research and investigation BEFORE making a decision. "We evaluated the vendor's security posture before engagement." "We assessed the risk before implementing this system." Proactive — gathering information to make informed decisions. Prerequisite to due care.

Examples: Vendor security questionnaire Risk assessment before project Background check before hiring Vulnerability scan before deployment

Due Care

Doing what a reasonable person would do to protect against harm — ONGOING reasonable precautions. "We maintain controls that a reasonable organization would maintain." Standard of care expected. Failure = negligence. Regulators ask: "Did you do what any reasonable organization would do?"

Examples: Keeping systems patched Annual security training Monitoring access to sensitive data Responding to known vulnerabilities

06Privacy ConceptsPII · PHI · Data Minimization

Privacy is about individuals' rights to control their personal information. Security enables privacy but they are distinct: security is about protecting data from unauthorized access; privacy is about ensuring data is only collected, used, and shared in ways people consent to and expect.

Types of Protected Personal Data

PII — Personally Identifiable Information

Any information that can directly or indirectly identify a specific individual. No universal legal definition — varies by jurisdiction. Protected by: GDPR, CCPA, state privacy laws.

Direct identifiers (alone = PII): Full name, SSN, passport number, driver's license, email, phone, biometric data, face photo Indirect identifiers (combined = PII): ZIP code + DOB + gender → often identifies a specific person IP address (often = PII under GDPR)

PHI — Protected Health Information

Any health information linked to an individual's identity. HIPAA-specific term. 18 identifiers defined by HHS. Any of the 18 + a health condition = PHI. Electronic PHI (ePHI) = PHI in electronic form → Security Rule applies.

HIPAA 18 identifiers include: Name, address, DOB, phone, fax, email, SSN, MRN, account numbers, certificate/license numbers, VINs, device identifiers, URLs, IPs, biometrics, face photos, any unique characteristic/code

Special Category Data (GDPR)

Requires additional protection and explicit consent under GDPR. Higher bar for lawful processing. Includes: racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data for identification, health data, sex life/sexual orientation.

Sensitive PII

US context — PII that poses greater risk if disclosed. Financial information (account numbers, credit cards), credentials (passwords, PINs), children's data, biometrics, location data that reveals movements. Triggers additional notification requirements and breach notification obligations in most jurisdictions.

Privacy Principles — Core Concepts

# Privacy by Design — build privacy in from the start: Proactive not reactive: address privacy BEFORE collecting data Privacy as default: most private setting is the DEFAULT Privacy embedded: not bolted on — integral to design Full functionality: privacy AND utility — not zero-sum End-to-end security: full lifecycle protection Transparency: open about policies and practices User-centric: respect user privacy — center the individual # Key Privacy Principles (from GDPR and international standards): Lawful basis: Cannot process personal data without legal justification GDPR lawful bases: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, legitimate interests Data minimization: Collect only what is NECESSARY for the stated purpose "We need an email to create an account — not your DOB" More data = more risk = more liability Purpose limitation: Data collected for one purpose → cannot be used for another "We collected email for shipping — cannot use for marketing without separate consent" Storage limitation: Keep data only as long as necessary for the stated purpose Retention schedules: when purpose fulfilled → delete Cannot keep "just in case it might be useful someday" Accuracy: Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date Mechanism for individuals to correct inaccurate data Regular reviews of data quality Security (Integrity and Confidentiality): Appropriate technical and organizational measures Protect against unauthorized access, disclosure, destruction Where security and privacy overlap

Privacy vs Security — The Distinction

Security

Protects data from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. Technical and procedural safeguards. CIA Triad: Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability. Asks: "Is the data protected from those who shouldn't have it?"

Privacy

Ensures data is collected, used, and shared only in ways the individual expects and consents to. Regulatory and rights-based framework. Asks: "Should we have this data? Are we using it appropriately? Does the individual have control?" Security enables privacy but doesn't guarantee it.

Secure but NOT private: Perfectly secure HR database But used to discriminate in hiring based on religion → Security ✓ Privacy ✗

07Data Subject Rights & Privacy RolesRights · DPO · Processor · Controller

Privacy laws grant individuals specific rights over their personal data and define organizational roles and responsibilities. Understanding these rights and roles is directly tested on Security+.

GDPR Data Subject Rights

ACCESS
Right of Access (Article 15)
Individual can request a copy of all personal data held about them. Must be provided within 30 days. Must include: what data, purpose, who it's shared with, retention period, source. Organizations must have a process to handle Subject Access Requests (SARs). Free of charge for first request.
ERASURE
Right to Erasure — "Right to be Forgotten" (Article 17)
Individual can request deletion of their personal data. Must be honored when: data no longer necessary for original purpose, consent withdrawn and no other lawful basis, individual objects and no overriding legitimate interest. Cannot be honored when: legal obligation to retain, public interest, legal claim. Most misunderstood right — not absolute.
PORTABILITY
Right to Data Portability (Article 20)
Individual can receive their data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format (CSV, JSON). Can request transfer directly to another provider. Applies when: processing based on consent or contract AND processing automated. Enables: switching banks/providers without losing your data history. Applies to data the individual provided — not derived/inferred data.
RECTIFICATION
Right to Rectification (Article 16)
Individual can request correction of inaccurate personal data. Must be completed within 30 days. Must also notify any third parties who received the inaccurate data. Organizations must have a mechanism for individuals to update their information. Especially important for: credit bureaus, healthcare records, government databases.
RESTRICTION
Right to Restriction of Processing (Article 18)
Individual can request that processing of their data is paused while another right is being exercised. Example: individual contests accuracy of data — while accuracy is verified, restrict processing. Doesn't delete data — puts it in a "suspended" state. Must notify individual before lifting restriction.
OBJECT
Right to Object (Article 21)
Individual can object to processing based on legitimate interests or for direct marketing. For direct marketing: absolute right — must stop immediately. For legitimate interests: must stop unless compelling overriding interest or legal claim. Must be told of this right at first point of contact. Opt-out mechanisms must be easy to use.
AUTOMATED
Right Regarding Automated Decision-Making (Article 22)
Individual can refuse fully automated decisions that significantly affect them (credit scoring, job applications, insurance pricing). Must be able to request: human review of the decision, ability to contest the decision, explanation of the logic used. Applies when: purely automated AND legally/similarly significant effect. The AI fairness right.

Privacy Roles — Controller vs Processor

# GDPR defines distinct roles — different obligations for each: Data Controller: WHO: Decides WHY and HOW personal data is processed Example: Your company that collects customer data Obligations: - Must have lawful basis for processing - Responsible for data subject rights fulfillment - Must ensure processors comply - Notifies DPA of breaches (72 hrs) - Accountability — can be fined directly Data Processor: WHO: Processes personal data ON BEHALF OF controller Example: Cloud provider, payroll vendor, email platform Obligations: - Only process per controller's instructions - Must have DPA with controller - Sub-processors need approval - Security measures (Article 32) - Notify controller of breaches "without undue delay" - Can also be fined directly under GDPR Joint Controllers: Two orgs JOINTLY decide why and how to process data Both are controllers — must have joint controller agreement Example: two companies jointly running a website # Example chain: Your company (controller) → payroll SaaS (processor) → payroll SaaS's cloud provider (sub-processor) Controller responsible for ensuring whole chain complies

Data Protection Officer (DPO)

🧑‍⚖️
DPO Role and Requirements
Mandatory under GDPR when: public authority/body, core activities involve large-scale systematic monitoring of individuals, OR core activities involve large-scale special category data. DPO must be: expert in data protection law, independent (cannot be instructed — cannot be dismissed for performing DPO duties), accessible to data subjects, registered with supervisory authority. Can be internal employee or external contractor. NOT personally liable for org's GDPR compliance — that's the controller's responsibility.

08Privacy-Enhancing TechnologiesAnonymization · Pseudonymization · Encryption

Privacy-enhancing technologies (PETs) are technical tools that reduce or eliminate the ability to identify individuals while preserving data utility for analysis, testing, or sharing.

Core Privacy Technologies

🎭 Anonymization GDPR no longer applies
Irreversibly removes all identifying information — cannot be re-linked to an individual with any reasonable effort. If truly anonymized, GDPR no longer applies (not personal data). Extremely difficult to achieve — most "anonymized" data can be re-identified. True anonymization: removes ALL 18 HIPAA identifiers + resistant to re-identification attacks. Requires k-anonymity testing to verify.
Before: "Jane Smith, 04/15/1985, 10001, Breast cancer, $45,200" After: "Patient A, 1985, 100xx, Cancer, $40,000-50,000" Challenge: ZIP+DOB+sex can re-identify 87% of Americans True anonymization: very hard — often achieves pseudonymization instead
🔑 Pseudonymization GDPR still applies
Replaces direct identifiers with pseudonyms (tokens, codes). Re-identification is possible if the key or mapping table is available. GDPR explicitly mentions pseudonymization as a safeguard — reduces risk but data is still personal data. More practical than full anonymization — utility preserved with reduced risk. Common uses: database tokenization, research datasets, testing environments.
Name: "Jane Smith" → Token: "USR-8847-XK" SSN: "123-45-6789" → Token: "TKN-9034-MN" DOB: "04/15/1985" → Token: "DOB-7721-QR" Key mapping stored SEPARATELY with strict access control Research team: gets tokenized data — cannot re-identify Security team: holds key — can re-identify if legally required GDPR still applies: key holder can re-identify → still personal data
🔢 Data Masking Non-prod environments
Replaces sensitive data with realistic but fake data. Unlike pseudonymization — no key to reverse it. Static masking: one-time transformation for non-production environments. Dynamic masking: mask data in real-time based on user role (support agent sees XXX-XXX-1234, billing sees full number). Ideal for: developer testing with realistic data, non-production copies of databases, support portals.
Production: John Smith | [email protected] | CC: 4532-9812-3456-7890 Dev/Test: Bob Jones | [email protected] | CC: 4532-0000-0000-0001 Data structure preserved — application tests work Real customer data: never in non-production environments Dynamic masking: credit card → **** **** **** 7890 (last 4 visible to agent)
📊 Differential Privacy Statistical privacy
Mathematical technique adding calibrated noise to datasets or query results. Enables statistical analysis of population data while preventing identification of individuals. Google and Apple use it to collect usage statistics from devices without identifying users. The privacy budget limits how many queries can be answered before individual data could be inferred.
🔒 Encryption Security + Privacy
Encrypting personal data reduces risk in case of breach — encrypted data is generally not "a breach" under many regulations (safe harbor provisions). GDPR: encryption can be a reason not to notify individuals if breached (data unintelligible). HIPAA: encrypted ePHI is "unsecured" only if encryption key is also compromised. Encryption = both security control AND privacy protection.
Encrypted breach safe harbor: GDPR: if breached data is encrypted → may not need to notify individuals (Article 34 exception) HIPAA: encrypted ePHI breach → not a reportable breach (as long as key not compromised) → Encryption reduces notification obligations → Makes breaches less damaging in regulatory terms
📋 Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA / DPIA) GDPR mandatory for high-risk
Systematic assessment of how a new project, system, or process will affect individual privacy. GDPR mandates a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) for high-risk processing activities (large-scale, systematic monitoring, special category data, new technologies). Done BEFORE implementing the new processing — proactive privacy protection. Involves: describing the processing, assessing necessity, identifying risks, determining mitigation measures.
When DPIA required (GDPR Article 35): Systematic evaluation of personal aspects (profiling) Large-scale processing of special category data Systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas (CCTV) New technologies with unclear risk profile DPIA output: Description of processing Necessity/proportionality assessment Risk identification Mitigation measures DPO consultation if high-risk remains
