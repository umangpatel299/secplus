01Risk Management OverviewIdentify · Assess · Treat · Monitor

Risk management is the systematic process of identifying, analyzing, and responding to threats that could harm the organization. Security is fundamentally about risk — you can't eliminate all risk, so you must understand which risks to accept, reduce, transfer, or avoid.

Core Risk Concepts

# Three foundational components of risk: RISK = THREAT × VULNERABILITY × IMPACT THREAT: Something that could cause harm (attacker, natural disaster, hardware failure) VULNERABILITY: Weakness that a threat can exploit (unpatched system, weak password, no MFA) IMPACT: Damage caused if threat exploits vulnerability (data breach, downtime, financial loss, reputation) # Examples: Threat: ransomware attacker Vulnerability: unpatched Windows Server (EternalBlue) Impact: all servers encrypted, 3-day outage Risk: HIGH — all three factors present Threat: earthquake Vulnerability: single data center in fault zone Impact: complete loss of infrastructure Risk: depends on likelihood of earthquake in your region # Reducing risk means reducing threat, vulnerability, OR impact Control: reduces vulnerability → lowers risk Insurance: reduces financial impact → lowers risk Off-site DR: reduces outage impact → lowers risk

Key Risk Terminology

Inherent Risk

The level of risk that EXISTS before any controls are applied. Raw risk. What you're naturally exposed to. Always the starting point of any risk assessment.

Inherent risk: System: Windows Server 2016 Internet-facing: yes Contains: customer PII Controls: NONE YET Inherent risk: CRITICAL

Residual Risk

The risk that REMAINS after controls are applied. Can never be zero. Organization must decide if residual risk is acceptable. If residual risk > risk appetite → need more controls.

After controls: Patched: yes → reduces exploitability WAF: deployed → reduces attack surface MFA: enabled → reduces credential attacks DLP: running → reduces data exfil impact Residual risk: MEDIUM (still some risk)

Control Risk

Risk that a control will fail or be bypassed. Controls are not perfect — they can fail, be misconfigured, or be circumvented. Example: MFA control risk = user approves MFA fatigue attack. Must account for control failure probability.

Risk Exposure

The total potential impact if a risk materializes. Financial, operational, reputational, legal dimensions. Quantified as: Annualized Loss Expectancy (ALE) for financial measurement. Used to justify security investment.

02Risk IdentificationAssets · Threats · Vulnerabilities

You can't manage risks you don't know about. Risk identification is the systematic process of discovering all potential threats, vulnerabilities, and their associated assets before they become incidents.

What to Identify — Three Layers

# Layer 1 — ASSETS (what needs protecting): Information assets: customer data, intellectual property, financial records System assets: servers, workstations, network devices, cloud instances People assets: key employees, contractors, leadership Physical assets: data centers, offices, equipment Process assets: business processes, supply chains, third parties # Layer 2 — THREATS (what could harm them): External: hackers, APTs, nation states, competitors, criminals Internal: disgruntled employees, negligent users, contractors Natural: earthquakes, floods, hurricanes, fire Technical: hardware failure, software bugs, power outages Third-party: vendor breach, supply chain attack, partner compromise # Layer 3 — VULNERABILITIES (weaknesses to exploit): Technical: unpatched systems, misconfigurations, weak crypto Process: no change management, no security awareness training People: untrained staff, weak passwords, over-privileged accounts Physical: unlocked doors, no badge access, no CCTV coverage

Risk Identification Methods

Asset Inventory

You can't protect what you don't know you have. Full inventory of hardware, software, data, cloud resources, and people. CMDB (Configuration Management Database). Automated discovery tools. Basis for all risk identification.

Vulnerability Scans

Automated identification of technical vulnerabilities. Nessus, Qualys, OpenVAS. Compares system configs against known CVE database. Shows exact weaknesses attackers could exploit. Regular cadence — weekly/monthly minimum.

Penetration Testing

Authorized exploitation of vulnerabilities. Finds what automated scans miss. Shows real-world exploitability not just theoretical. Results: proof-of-concept attacks with remediation guidance. Annual minimum for most organizations.

Threat Intelligence

External knowledge of active threats. CISA alerts, vendor threat reports, ISAC feeds, dark web monitoring. "Are attackers actively targeting organizations like us?" Contextualizes which vulnerabilities matter most right now.

Audit Findings

Internal and external audits identify control gaps. Compliance audits reveal where policy isn't being followed. Third-party risk assessments reveal vendor vulnerabilities. Audit findings → risk register entries.

Interviews & Workshops

Subject matter experts often know about risks that aren't documented anywhere. Business process owners, IT staff, executives. Structured interviews and risk workshops surface hidden operational and process risks.

03Risk Assessment & AnalysisQualitative · Quantitative · SLE · ALE

Risk assessment determines the probability of a risk occurring and its potential impact. Two fundamental approaches: qualitative (descriptive) and quantitative (numerical). Both have strengths and are used in different contexts.

Qualitative Risk Analysis

# Qualitative = descriptive categories, not exact numbers Likelihood ratings: Impact ratings: Rare (1) Negligible (1) Unlikely (2) Minor (2) Possible (3) Moderate (3) Likely (4) Major (4) Almost Certain (5) Catastrophic (5) Risk level = Likelihood × Impact Examples: Ransomware (healthcare org): Likelihood: Likely (4) — healthcare targeted frequently Impact: Catastrophic (5) — patient safety + HIPAA + revenue Risk score: 20 → CRITICAL Coffee machine power failure: Likelihood: Possible (3) Impact: Negligible (1) Risk score: 3 → LOW # Advantages: → Fast and easy — no financial data needed → Works when numbers are hard to estimate → Good for initial prioritization # Disadvantages: → Subjective — "likely" means different things to different people → Can't directly compare to cost of controls → "Major" impact doesn't tell you how many dollars

Quantitative Risk Analysis

# Quantitative = real dollar figures — must know these formulas: AV — Asset Value The monetary value of the asset being protected Example: CRM database = $2,000,000 (replacement + lost business) EF — Exposure Factor Percentage of asset value LOST if threat materializes (0-100%) Ransomware on CRM: 40% (data encrypted but backup exists) Complete hardware destruction: 100% (total loss) SLE — Single Loss Expectancy Expected loss from ONE occurrence of a risk Formula: SLE = AV × EF Example: $2,000,000 × 40% = SLE = $800,000 ARO — Annualized Rate of Occurrence How many times expected per YEAR (can be fraction) ARO = 1.0 → expected once per year ARO = 0.5 → expected once every two years ARO = 2.0 → expected twice per year Ransomware in healthcare: ARO = 0.3 (once every ~3 years) ALE — Annualized Loss Expectancy Expected annual financial loss from a specific risk Formula: ALE = SLE × ARO Example: $800,000 × 0.3 = ALE = $240,000 Using ALE to justify security investment: Current ALE (ransomware): $240,000/year Backup solution + EDR cost: $50,000/year New ALE with controls: $30,000/year (EF reduced to 5%) ALE reduction: $210,000/year Cost of control: $50,000/year Net benefit: $160,000/year savings ✓

Qualitative vs Quantitative — When to Use Each

Qualitative — Use When:

  • Quick initial prioritization needed
  • Financial data hard to estimate
  • Intangible impacts (reputation, morale)
  • Strategic-level decisions
  • Non-technical stakeholders involved
  • Time constraints — fast assessment needed

Quantitative — Use When:

  • Justifying security investment to finance/board
  • Comparing control cost to risk reduction
  • Insurance coverage decisions
  • Regulatory compliance documentation
  • Detailed risk analysis for major decisions
  • Financial impact must be precise
// Scenario — Using ALE to Justify Security Budget
# CISO presents to CFO: "Why should we spend $200K on security?" Without numbers: "Ransomware is a serious threat and we need to invest" CFO: "That's vague — rejected" With ALE calculation: Risk: Ransomware attack on financial systems AV = $5,000,000 (financial system value + recovery costs) EF = 60% (most data recoverable but 2 weeks downtime) SLE = $5,000,000 × 60% = $3,000,000 ARO = 0.25 (industry data: 1 attack per 4 years for firms our size) ALE = $3,000,000 × 0.25 = $750,000/year expected loss Proposed controls: EDR + backups + training = $200,000/year New EF: 10% (fast recovery from immutable backups) New ARO: 0.1 (better detection reduces successful attacks) New SLE: $5,000,000 × 10% = $500,000 New ALE: $500,000 × 0.1 = $50,000/year ALE reduction: $750,000 - $50,000 = $700,000/year saved Control cost: $200,000/year ROI: 350% — pay back in 4 months ✓ CFO: "Approved"

Risk Prioritization Factors

Severity × Exploitability

Not just theoretical impact — how easily can this actually be exploited? A critical vulnerability in an isolated system may be lower priority than a medium vulnerability in an internet-facing system. EPSS (Exploit Prediction Scoring System) helps — probability of exploitation in the wild.

Asset Criticality

Same vulnerability in a dev server vs production CRM has different priority. Asset criticality ranking determines which systems get patched first. Crown jewels (most critical assets) get priority treatment regardless of risk score.

Regulatory Exposure

Risks in regulated environments (cardholder data, PHI) get elevated priority due to legal consequences. A medium-risk control gap in PCI-DSS scope may be treated like HIGH due to compliance implications.

Threat Intelligence Context

CISA KEV (Known Exploited Vulnerabilities) — if a CVE is actively exploited in the wild, treat it as higher priority than its CVSS score suggests. Current threat landscape affects prioritization.

Risk Exceptions — When You Can't Fix Everything Now

📝

When a risk cannot be immediately remediated (budget, legacy system, technical constraints), the organization formally documents a risk exception. Must include: risk description, why it cannot be remediated, compensating controls in place, residual risk level, business owner acknowledgment, review date, and CISO/executive approval. Exceptions accepted above risk appetite require executive or board sign-off.

06Risk Tolerance & AppetiteHow Much Risk is OK?

Risk appetite and tolerance define how much risk an organization is willing to accept. These are strategic governance decisions made at the board level — they drive everything from security investment to risk treatment priorities.

Risk Appetite vs Risk Tolerance — The Distinction

# Risk Appetite — broad strategic statement: "The AMOUNT of risk an organization is WILLING to accept in pursuit of its objectives." Board-level decision. Strategic. Broad. Example: "We have LOW appetite for risks that could expose customer PII or result in regulatory fines." Example: "We have MODERATE appetite for operational risks to support business innovation speed." # Risk Tolerance — specific acceptable deviation: "The DEGREE of variation around the risk appetite that is acceptable in day-to-day operations." More specific. Measurable. Operational. Example: "We will not accept more than 5% of systems with critical unpatched vulnerabilities older than 30 days." Example: "Maximum acceptable downtime: 4 hours/year." Example: "No more than $50,000 in uninsured risk for any single risk event." # Analogy: Risk Appetite: "We are health-conscious people" (broad statement) Risk Tolerance: "We will eat dessert max 2x/week" (specific limit) # When residual risk > tolerance → must take action # When residual risk < tolerance → within acceptable range

Risk Appetite Spectrum

Risk Appetite Levels:
Risk Averse
Risk Neutral

Risk Appetite by Organization Type

Risk Averse (Low Appetite)

Healthcare, financial services, government, critical infrastructure. Strict regulations. Any breach = massive fines + patient/customer harm. Small risk tolerance. Heavy investment in controls. May sacrifice speed/innovation for safety.

Statement: "We do not accept any risk to patient safety or PHI privacy regardless of business benefit."

Risk Neutral (Moderate)

Typical mid-size enterprise. Balances security investment with business agility. Accepts some calculated risks. Prioritizes by potential impact. Most common organizational position.

Statement: "We accept risks where cost of control exceeds potential loss, if residual risk is below $100K ALE."

Risk Tolerant (Higher Appetite)

Startups, fast-growing tech companies, high-growth environments. Speed prioritized over perfect security. Accepts more risk to move fast. Still has limits — compliance floor. Compensates with agile response capability.

Statement: "We accept moderate technical risks to maintain development velocity — we respond quickly to incidents."

Risk Threshold — The Bright Lines

# Risk thresholds = hard limits that cannot be crossed: These risks are NEVER acceptable (zero tolerance): × Child safety violations × Knowingly violating data protection laws × Actions creating life-safety risk (hospital, utility) × Reputational risks that could destroy the business × Risks that exceed cyber insurance coverage Even if cost of control > ALE for these — we still control them Business units cannot override these thresholds Executive approval required for any exception # Everything else has a threshold — "we'll accept X but not X+1": Patching SLA: Critical vulns remediated within 14 days (threshold: no more than 2% exceptions per quarter) Availability: 99.9% uptime target (threshold: no single outage > 4 hours) Data breach: Financial impact < $500,000 per event (above this triggers board notification)

// Exam Keywords

07Risk Response StrategiesAvoid · Transfer · Mitigate · Accept

Once risks are identified and assessed, the organization must decide how to respond. There are four fundamental strategies — and knowing which one applies when is directly tested on Security+.

🚫
Strategy 1 — AVOID (Avoidance)
Eliminate the risk entirely by not doing the risky activity at all. "Don't store credit card data → no PCI-DSS scope." "Don't connect that system to the internet → no remote attack surface." Most effective — risk gone completely. But may mean giving up business opportunities. Used when risk > benefit of the activity.
Examples: Don't accept credit cards → no PCI-DSS risk Don't store SSNs → no identity theft risk for that data Don't connect legacy SCADA to internet → no remote attack Stop using vulnerable software → eliminate that CVE When to use: Risk is too high and activity is optional Regulatory exposure exceeds business value No cost-effective way to reduce the risk
📤
Strategy 2 — TRANSFER (Transference)
Shift the financial impact of the risk to a third party. Doesn't eliminate the risk — just who pays if it materializes. Cyber insurance is the classic example. Also: outsourcing to a vendor (they take on operational risk). Note: transferring financial risk doesn't transfer reputational risk. Still your problem if a breach occurs — just someone else pays.
Examples: Cyber insurance: insurer pays breach costs Cloud provider SLA: provider responsible for infrastructure Outsource payroll: vendor assumes data security risk Contract clause: vendor liable for their breach Important nuance: Transfer ≠ eliminate responsibility GDPR: you're still liable for your data processors Reputation: customers blame YOU, not your insurer Transfer = financial protection, not risk elimination
🔧
Strategy 3 — MITIGATE (Reduction)
Reduce the likelihood OR impact of the risk through security controls. Most common strategy in security. Doesn't eliminate risk — reduces it to an acceptable level. Controls can reduce: likelihood (MFA makes credential attacks less likely) or impact (backup reduces ransomware impact). Cost of control should be less than the ALE reduction achieved.
Reduce LIKELIHOOD: Patch management: reduces exploitability of CVEs MFA: reduces successful credential attacks Security awareness training: reduces phishing success Web Application Firewall: reduces SQL injection success Reduce IMPACT: Immutable backups: reduces ransomware damage Network segmentation: limits lateral movement blast radius DLP: reduces volume of data that can be exfiltrated Cyber insurance: reduces financial impact (also transfer) Incident response plan: reduces recovery time When to use: Most security controls are mitigation Cost of control < ALE reduction Risk can't be avoided or is too large to accept
✅
Strategy 4 — ACCEPT (Acceptance)
Acknowledge the risk exists and consciously decide to take no action — or minimal action. Appropriate when: cost of control exceeds potential loss, risk is within appetite, or risk is unavoidable. Must be a DOCUMENTED, CONSCIOUS decision — not just ignoring the risk. Two types: active acceptance (monitor it) and passive acceptance (just accept). Requires documented risk exception for anything above risk appetite.
Examples: Acknowledge small DDoS risk to public blog Accept risk of single laptop theft (MDM = remote wipe) Accept CVE in internal-only, non-critical system Requirements for proper acceptance: ✓ Documented in risk register ✓ Risk owner formally acknowledges ✓ Within risk appetite (or exception approved) ✓ Review date set — not permanent ✗ "We just haven't gotten to it yet" = NOT acceptance

Choosing the Right Strategy

# Decision framework: Is the risky activity ESSENTIAL to the business? NO → AVOID (eliminate the activity) YES → continue... Is the risk ABOVE risk appetite? NO → ACCEPT (document in register) YES → continue... Can the financial impact be shifted? YES → TRANSFER (insurance, contract) Partially → combine transfer + mitigate Is there a cost-effective control? YES → MITIGATE (implement control) NO → escalate for executive acceptance # Most risks use MULTIPLE strategies: Ransomware risk: MITIGATE: EDR + immutable backup + patching (reduce likelihood + impact) TRANSFER: cyber insurance (reduce financial impact) ACCEPT: residual risk (after controls, remaining risk is within appetite)
// Scenario — Legacy System Risk Decision
# Windows Server 2003 running critical manufacturing app — end of life 2015 # Cannot be patched — vendor won't update — but app is critical Risk assessment: Likelihood: HIGH (unpatched, many known exploits) Impact: CRITICAL (manufacturing line stops = $50K/hour) Score: CRITICAL Strategy evaluation: AVOID: don't use the server Can't — the manufacturing app only runs on this OS Replacement project: 18-month timeline, $2M cost TRANSFER: cyber insurance Insurance won't cover known unpatched systems Not viable for this risk MITIGATE (compensating controls): Isolate server: air-gapped VLAN with no internet Firewall: strict ACLs — only required ports to/from Monitor: IDS on the network segment Accounts: only one service account, no interactive login Physical: locked server room, limited physical access ACCEPT (residual risk after mitigation): Risk reduced from CRITICAL to MEDIUM Residual risk within tolerance for 18-month timeframe Exception documented + approved by CISO + CTO Review: quarterly + when replacement project completes Outcome: Accept with compensating controls + exception approval ✓

08Business Impact Analysis (BIA)RTO · RPO · MTTR · MTBF

The Business Impact Analysis (BIA) identifies which business functions are most critical, how quickly they must be restored, and the financial/operational impact if they're disrupted. BIA is the foundation of Business Continuity Planning (BCP) and Disaster Recovery (DR).

BIA Core Purpose

# BIA answers three fundamental questions: 1. WHAT business functions exist? All critical processes: order processing, patient care, payroll, customer service, manufacturing, trading... 2. WHAT HAPPENS if each function stops? Financial impact per hour/day of downtime Regulatory/legal exposure from outage Reputational damage potential Cascading effects on other functions 3. HOW QUICKLY must each function be restored? Not everything needs to come back immediately Prioritize: most critical first Resources allocated: based on BIA priorities # Output: prioritized list of critical business functions # with specific recovery time requirements # and quantified impact of downtime

Key BIA Terms — Exam Critical

RTO Recovery Time Objective Maximum acceptable time a system can be DOWN. "We can survive this function being unavailable for X hours." Sets the target recovery time. RTO = 4 hours means systems MUST be restored within 4 hours. Drives: backup frequency, DR site capability, staffing during incidents.
RPO Recovery Point Objective Maximum acceptable data LOSS measured in time. "We can afford to lose up to X hours/minutes of data." RPO = 1 hour means backups must happen at least every hour. Drives: backup frequency, replication strategy. RPO = 15 min requires near-continuous replication. RPO = 24 hours allows nightly backups.
MTTR Mean Time To Recover/Repair Average time to restore a system after failure. MTTR = how long it actually TAKES to fix things. Compare to RTO: if MTTR > RTO → you're not meeting your recovery objective. Used to measure DR capability and set realistic RTOs. Lower MTTR = better recovery capability.
MTBF Mean Time Between Failures Average time a component WORKS before failing. Used for: hardware reliability planning, predicting when replacements needed, availability calculations. Higher MTBF = more reliable hardware. Used in availability SLA calculations: Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR).
MTTF Mean Time To Failure Average lifespan of a non-repairable component before it fails completely. Used for: disposable components, HDDs, batteries. Unlike MTBF, MTTF is for items you replace not repair. Drives hardware replacement cycles and inventory planning.
MTD Maximum Tolerable Downtime Absolute maximum time a business function can be unavailable before the organization suffers catastrophic, unrecoverable harm. "After this point, we may not survive as a business." MTD is always ≥ RTO. RTO is the target; MTD is the hard deadline.

RTO vs RPO — The Critical Distinction

# Most tested BIA concepts — know the difference: TIME → Incident Last backup Restore complete occurs point │ │ │ ▼ ▼ ▼ ──────────────────────────────────────── │← ──── RPO ──── →│ │ │ │ │ │← ──────────── RTO ──────────── →│ RPO: How much data do we lose? (time between last backup and incident) RTO: How long are we down? (time from incident to full recovery) # Example — Hospital EHR System: Incident: ransomware at 02:14 AM Monday RPO = 15 minutes: Last backup: 02:00 AM → 14 minutes of data lost ✓ within RPO RTO = 4 hours: Recovery complete: 06:00 AM → 3 hrs 46 min ✓ within RTO # Key relationship: RTO + RPO determine: backup frequency + DR infrastructure cost Lower RTO/RPO = more expensive to achieve Financial system: RPO=0, RTO=minutes (very expensive) Archive system: RPO=24hr, RTO=72hr (very cheap)

  1. Identify all business functions
  2. Interview process owners — what happens if this stops?
  3. Quantify impact: $/hour of downtime, regulatory exposure, customer impact, cascading effects

Step 4-6: Define & Plan

  1. Set RTO and RPO for each function
  2. Assign criticality tiers
  3. Feed results into BCP/DR planning — determine infrastructure required to meet RTO/RPO
